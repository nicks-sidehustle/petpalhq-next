## How filtration actually works

### Filter type comparison

Hang-on-back filters are the easiest beginner recommendation when you want simple installation, easy media access, and straightforward maintenance. The best HOBs are the ones with **real media baskets**, not just a disposable cartridge slot, because you can separate sponge, biomedia, and optional chemical media. That is why AquaClear and Tidal work so well in an affiliate article: they are easier to explain, easier to upgrade, and easier to monetize responsibly. Canister filters win once tank size and bioload increase. They usually offer more media volume, cleaner aesthetics, and better long-term flexibility for 55- and 75-gallon tanks. Their tradeoff is cost and maintenance complexity. A canister is usually the more honest recommendation for a 55- or 75-gallon article than pretending a starter HOB will do the same job with the same convenience. ### Filter media explanation

ŅłĆentityŅłé["company","Aquarium Co-Op","aquarium retailer"]Ņłü and other aquarium education sources explain filtration in three buckets: **mechanical**, **biological**, and **chemical**. Mechanical media physically traps solid debris. Biological media gives beneficial bacteria a place to live. Chemical media adsorbs dissolved compounds like odors, discoloration, and some medication residues. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn11search1Ņłéturn11search4Ņłéturn11search7Ņłü

Biological filtration is the most important concept to tie back to your hub article. In an aquarium, fish waste and decaying organics produce ammonia. Beneficial bacteria then convert ammonia to nitrite and nitrite to nitrate. That is why biomedia matters so much: it provides stable surface area for the bacteria that make a tank safer during cycling and day-to-day operation. Without that biological capacity, a tank can look clear but still test badly. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn12search0Ņłéturn12search3Ņłéturn12search14Ņłéturn12search17Ņłü

The ideal beginner media stack is usually **coarse mechanical media first, biological media next, and optional chemical media last** in the flow path. That order catches solids early, protects biomedia from clogging, and lets carbon or Purigen do polishing work only after water has already passed through the sponge and biomedia stages. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn11search7Ņłéturn11search1Ņłü

Activated carbon deserves a careful explanation. It is useful, but it is not mandatory daily media in every freshwater tank. It is best framed as a **targeted tool** for tannins, odor, discoloration, photoshoot clarity, and post-treatment cleanup. Most hobbyists do not need to run it continuously, and it should be removed during medication because it can adsorb treatment. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn24search2Ņłéturn24search3Ņłéturn39search0Ņłéturn32search5Ņłéturn32search13Ņłü

Replacement cartridges are where many beginner articles go wrong. Cartridge systems are convenient, but replacing the entire cartridge can also discard bacteria growing on that media. ### Do I need activated carbon in my aquarium filter all the time?

### Why is biological filtration so important?

Because biological filtration is what helps the aquarium process toxic ammonia and nitrite. Beneficial bacteria colonize sponge and biomedia surfaces, then convert ammonia to nitrite and nitrite to nitrate. That is the functional core of a cycled freshwater aquarium. ### Is clear water proof that my aquarium is safe?

### Are sponge filters good enough by themselves?

### Should I replace all my filter media at once?

Usually, no. Replacing all media at once can discard a lot of beneficial bacteria.
