## Research-backed core guidance

In recirculating aquariums, filtration has three core jobs: remove suspended waste, provide stable surface area for nitrifying bacteria, and optionally remove specific dissolved compounds. Veterinary guidance from the entity["organization","Merck Veterinary Manual","veterinary reference"] says all recirculating systems have biologic and mechanical components and most also include a chemical component; home-aquarium guidance from entity["company","Aqueon","aquarium supplies brand"] defines the same three stages for freshwater hobby tanks. citeturn37view4turn30view0

Biological filtration is the load-bearing concept in this hub. Fish excrete ammonia, decomposing food adds more, and nitrifying microbes convert ammonia to nitrite and then nitrate. Nitrite is still toxic, and nitrate remains a chronic water-quality problem if it accumulates, especially for invertebrates and over the long term. That is why a tank can look clear but still be chemically unsafe. citeturn20view0turn37view5turn20view17

### How to explain flow rate, turnover, media capacity, and tank matching

**Flow rate** is the filter or pump’s rated water movement, usually in gallons per hour.  
**Turnover rate** is the rated flow divided by tank volume. A 200 GPH filter on a 40-gallon tank is a nominal 5x turnover. That math is simple; the hard part is that actual delivered flow is often lower once hoses, elbows, media, dirt load, and vertical lift are involved. LiveAquaria explicitly notes that head height, tubing turns, and clogged media reduce real flow, while older Fluval specifications distinguish pump output from lower in-system circulation with filtration media installed. citeturn25view0turn32view0turn30view2

**Media capacity** means usable internal space for sponge, floss, ceramic bio media, bio balls, carbon, or specialty resins. This matters because more media space usually means longer contact time, more bacterial habitat, and more flexibility in how you layer mechanical, biological, and chemical stages. Fluval’s 07 canister line markets “maximized media capacity” with multiple chambers; canisters and sumps physically hold far more media than most nano internals or small HOBs. citeturn27view0turn26view0turn21view6

**Maintenance frequency** is not one fixed universal number. Mechanical pads clog faster in messy tanks, activated carbon exhausts faster than ceramic bio media, and shrimp or planted tanks may need gentler, less disruptive service intervals. Fluval and Aqueon both give staged maintenance intervals by media type, and both caution against changing all media at once. citeturn22view4turn22view3turn21view0turn21view9

**Tank-size matching** should start with the manufacturer rating, then move one step more conservative if the tank will be heavily stocked, fed heavily, or house messy species. Fluval’s care guide explicitly advises using the next higher unit for heavily loaded aquariums; its AquaClear line also offers broad size and flow tiers from 5–20 gallon tanks up to 60–110 gallon tanks. citeturn23view2turn22view1

### Assumptions used in this hub

The turnover targets and “relative media capacity” labels in the comparison tables below are **editorial planning assumptions**, not universal rules. They synthesize manufacturer model ranges, veterinary system-design principles, and a credible hobbyist-education benchmark of roughly 4x average turnover for many aquariums, with slower-flow exceptions for bettas and planted tanks and stronger-flow exceptions for larger or messier fish. citeturn25view0turn21view3turn20view8turn20view9turn23view2

### Open questions and uncertainties

For planted tanks, circulation targets vary more than for basic community tanks because CO₂ retention, spray-bar design, lily-pipe placement, and plant mass can all change what “right flow” feels like in practice. Actual delivered GPH also varies by plumbing path and maintenance state, so any box-rating method is inherently approximate. Undergravel filters are especially setup-dependent because substrate choice and detritus accumulation can dramatically change results over time. citeturn25view0turn26view0turn30view2

## Maintenance routine, mistakes, and common questions

Filtration is only one part of water quality. Overfeeding can clog filters and raise ammonia and nitrite. Overstocking raises the daily nitrogen load. Testing tells you whether your actual husbandry fits your equipment. Water changes remove nitrate and dissolved waste that a typical freshwater filter does not permanently eliminate. citeturn21view10turn20view5turn18search1turn20view4turn33view0

### Maintenance checklist

**Daily**
- Check fish behavior, heater, and filter output.
- Feed conservatively; uneaten food should not sit in the tank.
- Remove obvious leftovers or dead plant matter. citeturn21view10turn33view0

**Weekly or every other week**
- Perform a partial water change; 10%–25% every 1–2 weeks is a practical baseline for many freshwater tanks.
- Scrape glass and spot-clean visible algae.
- Vacuum exposed debris where appropriate.
- Test ammonia, nitrite, nitrate, and pH more often in new, unstable, sick, or newly stocked tanks. citeturn33view0turn20view16turn18search1turn20view17

**Monthly**
- Rinse mechanical media in removed aquarium water if flow is dropping.
- Clean intake strainers, uplift tubes, and impellers.
- Replace exhausted chemical media on its shorter cycle.
- Disturb biological media only lightly, and only as needed to restore flow. citeturn21view0turn22view3turn22view4turn28search5

**Every few months**
- Replace worn floss, polishing pads, or heavily compressed foam.
- Replace only part of biological media if the manufacturer recommends periodic replacement.
- Deep-clean hoses or hidden plumbing on canisters and sumps. citeturn22view4turn29view0turn22view3

This is a baseline schedule, not a one-size-fits-all law. Goldfish, messy cichlids, and heavily fed tanks usually need tighter intervals. Mature, lightly stocked planted tanks can often stay stable on gentler service, as long as testing and fish behavior agree. citeturn20view9turn25view0turn20view16

### Beginner mistakes section

The most expensive filtration mistake is not buying the “wrong” brand. It is misunderstanding what the filter is responsible for.

- **Replacing all media at once.** This can dump too much beneficial bacteria at one time and destabilize ammonia and nitrite control.
- **Rinsing biological media in chlorinated tap water.** Manufacturers repeatedly direct users to rinse biological media in aquarium water.
- **Underfiltering for the bioload.** Box ratings are a starting point, not a promise.
- **Assuming more GPH is always better.** Bettas, shrimp, fry, and many planted layouts need gentler flow.
- **Relying on filtration instead of water changes.** Biofilters convert waste; they do not make nitrate and dissolved organics disappear.
- **Overfeeding and then blaming the filter.** Uneaten food directly fouls water and reduces filter efficiency.
- **Skipping testing during cycling, after deep cleaning, or after adding fish.** The nitrogen cycle is invisible until it fails. citeturn22view3turn22view4turn28search5turn25view0turn21view3turn20view8turn20view4turn21view10turn33view0turn18search1

### FAQ section

**Do I need chemical filtration all the time?**  
Not necessarily. Mechanical and biological filtration are foundational; chemical media is often situational and used for odor, discoloration, targeted chemistry problems, or medication cleanup. citeturn37view4turn30view0turn20view14

**Is a canister filter always better than a hang-on-back filter?**  
No. Canisters usually offer more media volume and customization, but HOB filters are simpler, cheaper, and plenty capable for many community tanks. “Better” depends on tank size, stock, service tolerance, and flow needs. citeturn26view0turn27view0turn22view1turn15search1

**How often should I replace filter media?**  
Chemical media usually changes fastest. Mechanical media changes when clogged or degraded. Biological media should usually be preserved, rinsed in tank water, and only replaced in part when necessary. citeturn22view3turn22view4turn28search5turn21view0

**Why did ammonia or nitrite spike after I cleaned my filter?**  
Common causes are overcleaning biomedia, replacing too much media at once, adding too many fish too quickly, or feeding too much for a still-maturing biofilter. citeturn20view3turn33view0turn20view0

**What filter is safest for shrimp?**  
Air-driven sponge filters are the default safest option, and Aqueon’s shrimp guide also recommends intake sponges on HOB or canister filters to stop shrimp from getting pulled in. citeturn20view8turn20view13

**Can filtration replace water changes?**  
No. Typical freshwater filtration converts ammonia to nitrate; it does not eliminate the need to export nitrate and dissolved waste with partial water changes. citeturn20view4turn33view0turn20view16

**What should a beginner test routinely?**  
At minimum: pH, ammonia, nitrite, and nitrate. New tanks, tanks after a big cleaning, and tanks with sick fish or recent stocking changes should be tested more frequently. citeturn18search1turn7search14turn20view17

**Do planted tanks need less filtration?**  
They usually need gentler circulation and less disruptive surface agitation, but they still need regular maintenance, debris control, and stable filtration. citeturn25view0turn20view16turn23view3
