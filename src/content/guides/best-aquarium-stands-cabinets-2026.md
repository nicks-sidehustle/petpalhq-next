## Why Weight Capacity Is The Only Spec That Really Matters

Start with the arithmetic, because it decides everything else. Water weighs roughly 8.34 pounds per gallon, so the water alone in a 75-gallon tank is about 626 pounds. Add the glass tank (a dry 75-gallon is around 89 pounds), a couple of inches of substrate, rock or driftwood, and a filter full of water, and a fully set-up 75-gallon comes in near 850 pounds — some references put a heavily loaded reef closer to 965. A 55-gallon runs a bit over 600 pounds loaded, a 40-gallon lands around 450 to 500, a 29-gallon about 290 to 320, and even a modest 10-gallon is 110 to 120 pounds once it is filled.

Now put those numbers next to the stands. The rule from every sizing guide is the same: the stand must be rated above the tank's filled weight, and its top must match the tank's footprint so the frame carries the bottom edges evenly. That is why the 4ever2buy cabinet's 1100-pound rating over an 850-pound loaded 75-gallon is comfortable, while the Herture stand's 330-pound rating over a 290-to-320-pound loaded 29-gallon is tight enough that we steer heavy-aquascape buyers up a size. Buy for the load, not the look.

There is a floor-loading question too, and it is not paranoia. Most US residential floors are engineered for roughly 40 to 50 pounds per square foot, and a loaded 75-gallon concentrates hundreds of pounds on a footprint barely four feet by a foot and a half. Position a big tank against a load-bearing wall where the joists are strongest, and if your floor is older, upstairs, or feels springy, get a contractor's eyes on it before you fill anything over 40 gallons. ## Metal Frame Versus Cabinet: Moisture Is The Real Divide

Once capacity is settled, the build choice comes down to a wet-hobby reality: what happens when water gets on the stand. Cabinet stands are made of MDF panels over a metal or wood frame, and MDF swells, softens, and loses structural strength when it soaks. In a hobby defined by splashes, overflows, and condensation, that is a genuine long-term risk, which is why the powder-coated all-metal TOCRETOARE stand rates highest on our build factor — there is simply no MDF on it to damage. An open metal frame shrugs off the water a cabinet has to be protected from.

The cabinets earn their place a different way: storage and cord management. The 4ever2buy cabinet and the VOWNER cabinet both hide the heater, filter, and supplies behind doors and route power through built-in outlets, which turns a tangle of cords into a clean console. The trade you are making is convenience and looks in exchange for keeping the cabinet dry — wipe spills immediately, seal or line the top if you overflow often, and never let the stand sit in standing water. Choose a cabinet if you want the tidy furniture look and will respect the MDF; choose the metal stand if you would rather not have to think about water at all.

Whichever you pick, level it before the tank goes on. A stand that is even slightly out of level twists the tank and concentrates stress on one corner of the glass, and that is a crack-and-leak setup no capacity rating can save you from. Use the adjustable feet, check it with a spirit level front-to-back and side-to-side while it is empty, and confirm the stand's top dimensions match the tank's base so the bottom rim is fully supported.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much weight does an aquarium stand actually need to hold?**

A: More than the tank's filled weight, with room to spare. Freshwater weighs about 8.34 pounds per gallon, so a filled 75-gallon runs roughly 850 pounds with substrate and equipment, a 55-gallon a bit over 600, a 40-gallon around 450 to 500, a 29-gallon about 290 to 320, and a 10-gallon 110 to 120. Add up your specific tank's water, glass, substrate, rock, and gear, then buy a stand rated comfortably above that total. A stand run right at its rating has no safety margin, and the failure you are guarding against is hundreds of pounds of water hitting your floor.

**Q: Can I put a fish tank on regular furniture instead of an aquarium stand?**

A: It is risky, and we do not recommend it for anything but the smallest tanks. A dresser or bookshelf is rated — if it is rated at all — for evenly spread household loads, not for hundreds of pounds concentrated on a tank's footprint, and it is usually not sized so the tank's bottom edges are supported. Ordinary furniture also is not built to shrug off the water an aquarium sheds. A purpose-made stand like the TOCRETOARE stand or a rated cabinet matches both the footprint and the load, which is exactly what generic furniture does not.

**Q: Is a metal aquarium stand better than a wood or MDF cabinet?**

A: For moisture resistance, yes. A powder-coated metal frame like the TOCRETOARE stand has no MDF to swell, so it tolerates the splashes and humidity of a fish room better over the years. A cabinet stand like the 4ever2buy cabinet or the VOWNER cabinet trades that for enclosed storage and built-in outlets, which is a real convenience — you just have to keep the MDF dry, wipe spills fast, and never let it sit in standing water. Pick metal if you want to stop worrying about water; pick a cabinet if you want hidden storage and will respect the material.

**Q: Will my floor hold a 75-gallon aquarium?**

A: Usually, if it is placed correctly, but it is worth checking. Most US residential floors are engineered for about 40 to 50 pounds per square foot, and a loaded 75-gallon near 850 pounds concentrates a lot of weight on a small area. Put the tank against a load-bearing wall where the joists are strongest, run it parallel to the joists rather than out into the middle of a span when you can, and if the room is upstairs, the house is old, or the floor feels springy, have a contractor confirm it before you fill anything over 40 gallons.

**Q: Do I need a stand rated for exactly my tank size, or can I buy bigger?**

A: Buy for the tank's footprint, and a higher weight rating is only ever a bonus. The stand's top needs to match the tank's base so the bottom edges are fully supported — a 40-gallon on the TOCRETOARE stand, a 20-gallon on the Herture stand, a 10-gallon on the MZNZ nano stand. Within that footprint match, more rated capacity is never a problem; it is the headroom that makes the filled-weight question boring. What you must not do is go the other way and put a larger, heavier tank on a stand sized and rated for a smaller one.
