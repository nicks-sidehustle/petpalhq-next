## Heat, UVB, and Safety: Where Kits Put Geckos at Risk

Heat and UVB are the two places this category can genuinely hurt an animal, and the risk is concentrated in what the boxes leave out rather than what they include.

The thermostat is non-negotiable. No product in this guide ships with one, and an unregulated heat mat can climb to burn-inducing surface temperatures with a gecko sitting on the glass above it. Wire every mat to a thermostat with the probe placed on the warm-side floor, targeting roughly 90 degrees Fahrenheit, and size the mat to cover about one-third of the floor so the animal can move across a real gradient to regulate its own temperature. The bundled analog gauges that kits include are not a substitute for measurement — ReptiFiles flags them as notoriously inaccurate, and a temperature error is a direct animal-welfare risk. A digital probe thermometer costs little and removes the guesswork.

Lighting has its own traps. ReptiFiles flags colored bulbs and heat rocks as unsafe and unnecessary, full stop. If supplemental night heat is genuinely needed — meaning the room drops below 60 degrees Fahrenheit — use a lightless source such as a ceramic heat emitter; otherwise, nighttime heating is overkill and potentially harmful. On the UVB side, the Exo Terra starter kit's Day and Night LED produces no UVB at all, and the expert recommendation is to add a low-output, shade-dweller-strength UVB fixture rather than a high-percentage desert bulb at close range. Our [reptile UVB bulb guide](/guides/best-reptile-uvb-bulbs-2026) breaks down which fixtures fit screen-top tanks like these.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What size tank does an adult leopard gecko need?**

The standard we anchor to comes from ReptiFiles: no smaller than 36 by 18 by 18 inches, with at least 4.5 square feet of floor space. The keeper community broadly agrees — Folium Biosciences reports 40 gallons as the widely accepted minimum for an adult — while MyPetReptiles publishes 20 gallons as the absolute floor for a single gecko. Floor area is the number to optimize, since leopard geckos are terrestrial and use ground space rather than height. ReptiFiles adds that larger, enriched enclosures encourage more exercise, which keeps geckos healthier and supports longer lifespans.

**Are leopard gecko starter kits actually worth the money?**

Mostly no, with one partial exception. ReptiFiles' position is blunt — reptile kits ship with notoriously inaccurate analog gauges and need serious changes to be anywhere close to functional. The Zilla 10-gallon kit illustrates the failure mode: undersized floor, non-adjustable heat, and a thermostat purchase required anyway, so nearly everything gets replaced. The exception is the Exo Terra starter kit, the only species-specific leo kit live on Amazon. Its terrarium, heat mat, hide, and supplements are genuinely usable for a juvenile; you still must add a thermostat, UVB, and a digital thermometer.

**Do leopard geckos need UVB lighting?**

The expert guidance we synthesized treats low-output UVB as the modern recommendation rather than an optional extra. The Pet Enthusiast's review of the Exo Terra kit states plainly that its included LED offers no UVB and that a proper UVB source must be added. The form factor matters: the recommendation is a low-output, shade-dweller-strength fixture, not a high-percentage desert bulb mounted at close range. Every 36-inch pick in this guide has a screen top that accepts an overhead UVB fixture directly, so adding correct lighting is a purchase decision, not a modification project.

**Do I need a thermostat with a reptile heat mat?**

Yes — this is the least negotiable purchase in the entire setup. No tank or kit in this guide includes a thermostat, and an unregulated heat mat can reach burn-inducing surface temperatures beneath a resting gecko. Wire every mat to a thermostat with the probe on the warm-side floor, targeting roughly 90 degrees Fahrenheit. Forum keepers on Geckos Unlimited make the same point about bundled kit mats: they run non-adjustable out of the box and need a controller regardless. Budget $20 to $35 for a basic unit, and treat it as part of the tank's real price.

**Is a 10-gallon tank big enough for a leopard gecko?**

Not for an adult. A 10-gallon floor is roughly half of the 40-gallon minimum the keeper community accepts, and well under ReptiFiles' 4.5-square-foot floor-space standard. A 10-gallon or 15-gallon enclosure works as temporary housing for a juvenile — that is exactly the role the Exo Terra starter kit plays in this guide — but the upgrade to a 36-inch enclosure should land within the first year. Buying the adult-size tank once is cheaper than buying a small kit, replacing its components, and then buying the big tank anyway.

**What do I have to add to a leopard gecko kit to make it safe?**

Three things, in priority order. First, a thermostat for the heat mat, with the probe on the warm-side floor at roughly 90 degrees Fahrenheit — the mat should cover about one-third of the floor so the gecko can thermoregulate. Second, a digital probe thermometer to replace the bundled analog gauge, which experts flag as notoriously inaccurate. Third, a low-output UVB fixture, since the kit's LED produces none. For bare tanks, extend the list with warm- and cool-side hides and appropriate substrate. Skip colored bulbs and heat rocks entirely — ReptiFiles flags both as unsafe and unnecessary.
