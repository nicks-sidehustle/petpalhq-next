      The Arcadia GoldenSun is the editorial synthesis pick for keepers who want the heat quality that current basking science favors. Arcadia's lighting guide, authored under John Courteney-Smith, MRSB, makes the case that halogen basking lamps emit a larger fraction of short-wavelength IR-A than standard incandescent or tungsten bulbs. IR-A penetrates deeper into tissue and more closely mimics the spectral profile of sunlight reaching a basking reptile. The LafeberVet article by Frances Baines and the Journal of Zoo and Aquarium Research UV-Tool paper both frame basking output around a species' natural sunlight-use pattern, which is the same logic that puts a high-IR-A halogen at the top of this list.

      Heat output and safety carry the next-largest weight, and the GoldenSun earns it with a wide-beam metal reflector that spreads warmth across a usable platform rather than a pinprick hot spot. That matters for animals that bask with the whole body flat — bearded dragons, tortoises, and similar desert species — where a broad, even warm zone beats a narrow scorching one. The 50W, 75W, and 100W options let you size the bulb to the species' basking-surface target instead of guessing.

      What the spec sheet doesn't tell you: Arcadia's own documentation states that halogen basking lamps should be run on a dimming thermostat, not an on/off one. The repeated on/off cycling of an on/off thermostat stresses the filament and burns a halogen out fast; a dimming thermostat varies power smoothly and protects the bulb. So the GoldenSun's real cost is not just the bulb — it is the bulb plus a compatible dimming controller. Our [reptile thermostat guide](/guides/best-reptile-thermostats-2026) covers the dimming-versus-pulse decision in detail, and pairing this lamp with the right controller is the difference between a bulb that lasts and one that fails in weeks.

Mistaking a basking bulb for a UVB lamp is the single most common beginner error in the source set, and it is a serious welfare gap because UVB drives vitamin D3 synthesis. Pair this lamp with a separate UVB fixture sized to the species, and confirm the basking-surface temperature with an infrared temperature gun rather than trusting the wattage label.

For most reptile keepers, the first decision about a heat lamp is not "how many watts?" — it is understanding what a basking lamp does and does not do. The Merck Veterinary Manual, the Association of Reptile and Amphibian Veterinarians, and Frances Baines' LafeberVet article all converge on the same point: a basking heat lamp supplies heat, visible light, and UVA, but it does not supply UVB. ## Wattage, basking temperature, and species targets — set the heat to the animal

The wattage on the box is not a temperature. The basking-surface temperature a reptile actually experiences depends on the bulb's wattage, the distance from the bulb to the basking surface, the fixture's reflector, and the ambient room temperature. The correct method, consistent across ReptiFiles and the veterinary references, is to choose a target surface temperature for the species, then select and adjust the wattage to hit it — verified with an infrared temperature gun at the basking spot.

Species targets differ widely, and matching the heat to the animal is the whole game. Bearded dragons need roughly 100–110°F at the basking surface, with juveniles toward the higher 105–110°F end. Tortoises and other desert baskers want about 95–100°F. Leopard geckos are not true baskers and rely mostly on belly heat; a warm spot around 90–95°F is appropriate. Ball pythons sit lower at roughly 88–92°F. At the far end, crested geckos do best cool — around 72–78°F and never much above 80°F — which means an over-powered basking lamp can actively harm a low-zone species. The Journal of Zoo and Aquarium Research UV-Tool and Frances Baines' Ferguson Zone framework formalize this idea: match basking output to a species' natural sunlight-use pattern, not to its body size. Baines and colleagues' preliminary, research-stage work suggests basking zones may target roughly 250–350 W/m² of IR-A, a figure to treat as an active research direction rather than a settled specification.

## Halogen vs incandescent, deep domes, and dimming thermostats — the three choices that matter

Three technical choices separate a safe basking setup from a risky one, and the source set is clear on each.

**Halogen versus incandescent.** Halogen basking lamps emit a higher fraction of short-wavelength IR-A than standard incandescent or tungsten bulbs. IR-A penetrates deeper into tissue and more closely mimics sunlight, which is why Arcadia Reptile, The Bio Dude, and Reptiles Magazine all favor halogen as the better basking technology. Arcadia states its halogens give roughly 30% more heat and light than a standard tungsten lamp — a manufacturer claim worth treating as such, but one that aligns with the broader consensus that halogen heat quality beats plain incandescent. An incandescent like the Zoo Med Repti Basking Spot is still a perfectly functional, budget-friendly choice; it simply sits a tier below halogen on heat quality and service life.

**Why deep domes prevent burns.** A deep-dome fixture fully recesses the bulb inside an extended reflector so the hot glass cannot protrude toward a climbing reptile. A shallow dome leaves the bulb exposed at the rim, where an animal scaling the screen can press against it. For extra protection, a wire safety guard or cage can be added over the basking lamp. Equally important and easy to overlook: the fixture must use a ceramic socket rated above the bulb's wattage. A 100W bulb belongs in a fixture rated to 150–160W; plastic sockets and under-rated domes are a melt and fire hazard, not a place to save money.

**Dimming versus on/off thermostats.** Halogen and incandescent basking bulbs must run on a dimming thermostat, not an on/off one. An on/off thermostat slams full power on and off repeatedly, and that cycling stresses the filament and burns these bulbs out fast. A dimming thermostat varies the power smoothly to hold the target temperature, which protects the bulb and produces a steadier basking zone. This is the single most common reason a basking bulb fails prematurely. Our [reptile thermostat guide](/guides/best-reptile-thermostats-2026) covers the dimming-versus-pulse decision in depth, and it is the companion purchase to every bulb in this guide. The ARAV notes that a large share of reptile health problems seen in clinics trace to improper environmental management, including incorrect temperatures — which is the strongest possible argument for treating the thermostat and the infrared thermometer as core husbandry equipment, not optional accessories.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What wattage basking bulb do I need to hit my reptile's basking temperature?**
A: There is no fixed wattage-to-temperature answer, because the basking-surface temperature depends on wattage, the distance from the bulb to the basking surface, the fixture's reflector, and the room temperature. Set the target first — bearded dragons around 100–110°F at the surface, tortoises about 95–100°F, leopard geckos a 90–95°F warm spot, ball pythons roughly 88–92°F — then choose a wattage and adjust it until an infrared temperature gun reads the target at the basking spot. A 50–100W halogen covers most common enclosures, but always confirm by measuring the surface rather than trusting the box.

**Q: Do reptile basking heat lamps also provide UVB, or do I need a separate UVB light?**
A: A basking heat lamp provides heat, visible light, and UVA only — it does not provide UVB. This is the most common beginner mistake, and it is a real welfare problem because UVB drives vitamin D3 synthesis and calcium metabolism. You need a separate UVB source matched to the species, in addition to the basking lamp. The one partial exception across reptile lighting generally is a mercury-vapor bulb, which combines UVB and heat, but a standard halogen or incandescent basking bulb is heat-and-light only.

**Q: Should I use a dimming thermostat or an on/off thermostat with a halogen basking bulb?**
A: Use a dimming thermostat. Halogen and incandescent basking bulbs have filaments that are stressed by the repeated full-power on/off cycling of an on/off thermostat, which burns them out rapidly. A dimming thermostat varies the power smoothly to hold the target temperature, protecting the bulb and producing a steadier basking zone. On/off and pulse-proportional thermostats are appropriate for non-light heat sources such as ceramic heat emitters and heat mats, but not for light-emitting basking bulbs.

**Q: Are colored red or blue "night" heat bulbs safe for reptiles?**
A: Herp consensus discourages them. Reptiles can perceive red and blue light, so a colored "night" bulb disrupts their day/night cycle and sleep rather than providing invisible heat. If a room genuinely gets too cold at night, supplemental heat should come from a non-light source such as a ceramic heat emitter or a deep heat projector, ideally on a thermostat. For most setups in a normally heated home, no nighttime heat source is needed at all, and a natural temperature drop overnight is appropriate.

**Q: Why does a deep-dome fixture prevent burns, and what wattage should the fixture be rated for?**
A: A deep-dome fixture fully recesses the bulb inside an extended reflector so the hot glass cannot protrude toward a climbing reptile that scales the screen top, and a wire safety guard can add further protection. Just as important, the fixture must use a ceramic socket rated above the bulb's wattage — a 100W bulb belongs in a fixture rated to 150–160W. Plastic sockets and under-rated domes are a melt and fire hazard, so always check the fixture's wattage rating before fitting a bulb.
