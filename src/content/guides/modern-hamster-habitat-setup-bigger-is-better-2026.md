A modern hamster setup goes wrong at the pet store, before any bedding is bought. A first-time owner sees a bright plastic cage with tubes and buys it in good faith, picturing a happy pet in a colorful playground. What a hamster actually needs is the opposite of that module tower. The hamster-welfare community's current housing standards inverted the old pet-store picture entirely: a habitat is floor space, deep bedding, and a wheel the spine fits, not a stack of small compartments. Bigger is not a luxury position here. It is the baseline the r/hamsters-class community now treats as the welfare floor, and this guide is built on it.

Two ideas carry the whole setup. The first is continuous floor. A hamster is a ground-dwelling burrower that roams, forages, and digs, so unbroken floor area matters far more than tiers or tube height. The second is depth. Bedding deep enough to tunnel into is enrichment a hamster cannot live well without. The tube-and-module cages sold as starters fail on both counts, and on airflow besides, which is why the community stopped recommending them.

Two more choices are welfare decisions, not preferences. A wheel is sized right only when the hamster runs with a flat back; the wrong size curves the spine every night. And hamsters clean their coats in fine sand, never in water, which strips their oils and chills a small animal fast. The picks below provision that setup in the order you need it — the habitat first, then the wheel, the deep bedding, the sand bath, the hide, and the chews. Where a claim rests on a maker's listing or community consensus, that is named rather than asserted, because PetPalHQ does not run a testing lab.

One rule sits above the gear and never bends. Syrian hamsters live alone, always, and most dwarfs do too, because cage-mates fight, sometimes to the death. No amount of floor space makes cohabitation safe for a Syrian. This guide's honest job is to stop the wrong first purchase before it happens, and the wrong purchase is almost always the small pet-store starter cage. The right first habitat is bigger than the shelf suggests and costs more, and that is the truth a new owner deserves before the money is spent, not after.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why is the pet-store starter cage such a common mistake?**
A: Because it fails on the one measure that matters most — continuous floor space — and it fails quietly. The bright plastic cages with tubes and levels look like a playground, but the actual floor is small and broken up by ramps and modules, so a hamster cannot roam or burrow properly. Owners rarely see the harm directly, which is what makes it common. The signs show up as stress behaviors instead: bar-biting, monkey-barring across the roof, and repetitive pacing, which the community reads as a cage that is too small. The tubes also trap stale air and cannot hold deep bedding. A habitat built on unbroken floor and a deep base prevents the problem the starter cage creates.

**Q: What size wheel does a Syrian hamster actually need?**
A: Big enough that the hamster runs with a flat back, which for a Syrian means the eleven- to twelve-inch class. The test is the posture, not the label on the box. If the hamster's back arches upward as it runs, the wheel is too small and its spine is being curved every night. The safe move is to go larger, because an oversized wheel causes no harm at all while an undersized one does. Ignore listings that market a wheel for dwarfs and stop there — a size that suits a Syrian suits a dwarf too. Watch the animal run for a few seconds and let its back, flat or arched, make the decision for you.

**Q: Can two hamsters live together?**
A: For Syrians, no — never, in any cage at any size. Syrians are solitary and territorial, and two housed together will fight, sometimes fatally, no matter how much floor space they have. Most dwarf species should also be kept alone; the occasional exceptions demand deep experience, huge space, and a fast plan to separate them, which puts them well outside a first setup. The dangerous trap is seeing babies housed together at the store and assuming it lasts — young siblings tolerate each other briefly, then turn on each other as they mature. The safe rule for a first-time owner is simple and firm: one hamster, one cage, every time.

**Q: How deep should the bedding be, and what kind?**
A: Six inches or more of packable, dust-free paper bedding is the depth the community treats as the standard. Depth is the point, because a hamster burrows and needs substrate it can dig real tunnels through rather than skate across. Paper bedding packs and holds a tunnel shape, so it beats loose scatter for a burrow. It has to be dust-free, because a hamster lives with its nose in it, and fragrance-free, because scent masks the ammonia smell that tells you a cleanout is due. Spot-clean soiled patches between full changes rather than stripping the whole burrow each time. One large bag is where the deep layer starts for a big cage, not where it ends.

**Q: Do hamsters need a water bath?**
A: No — a water bath is actively harmful, and a hamster should never be given one. Water strips the natural oils that keep a hamster's coat healthy and chills a small animal dangerously fast. Hamsters clean themselves in fine, dry sand instead, rolling and digging to work it through the coat, so a sand bath is the correct tool. Use a fine, low-dust sand — reptile sand or a dust-free chinchilla-grade sand works well — and skip dusty "bathing powder," which can irritate the airways. Offer the sand in a shallow box and leave it in the cage, since a hamster will use it on its own schedule. If a hamster gets something sticky on its coat, spot-clean the patch by hand rather than bathing the whole animal.
