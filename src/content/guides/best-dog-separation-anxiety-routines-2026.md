whenNotToBuy: |
  Clinical separation anxiety is not a product problem. The Merck Veterinary Manual describes canine separation-anxiety pharmacotherapy that includes FDA-approved fluoxetine and clomipramine formulations used alongside reward-based behavior modification. ASPCA explicitly recommends a structured desensitization and counter-conditioning program for moderate or severe cases. ASPCA also notes that medication can be very helpful, especially in severe cases. If your dog injures themselves trying to escape, panics seconds after you leave, refuses food when alone, eliminates from distress, drools heavily, vocalizes for long periods, or destroys exits, escalate to a veterinarian or a board-certified veterinary behaviorist. Do not add more products to a plan that is not yet working.

  Skip every bark collar, shock device, citronella collar, ultrasonic deterrent, prong collar, and choke chain. AVSAB's position statement on humane training is explicit: aversive methods, including shock/electronic collars, prong collars, choke chains, leash corrections, and other punishment methods, should not be used. In separation anxiety specifically, barking is a distress signal. Suppressing it does not treat panic. It can damage the dog's welfare and your relationship.

  Skip "anti-anxiety" supplements that claim to cure separation anxiety, replace medication, or stop panic naturally. The Merck Veterinary Manual and ASPCA both treat separation anxiety as a behavior disorder that may need medication — calming supplements are not medication. Avoid supplements that make disease-treatment claims, do not list dosing by weight, or do not display a National Animal Supplement Council (NASC) seal.

  Skip punishment-based devices entirely: scat mats, compressed-air punishers, shaker cans, water sprays, and devices that punish door scratching or barking. The Merck Veterinary Manual's behavior-treatment chapter favors environmental management and reward-based behavior modification; punishment risks increasing fear, hiding warning signs, and worsening the underlying panic.

  Do not crate a panicking dog to protect furniture. Some crate-trained dogs settle in a crate during mild departures, but for many dogs with barrier distress, confinement intensifies panic. ASPCA's separation-anxiety guidance is clear that the crate is not a solution for confinement-related distress. If your dog is destroying the crate from inside, scratching teeth or claws bloody, or escaping, you are looking at a welfare problem — call a veterinary behaviorist.

Clinical separation anxiety is a panic-related behavior disorder. It is not a training failure and not a product problem. The Merck Veterinary Manual is explicit on treatment. Canine separation-anxiety pharmacotherapy includes FDA-approved fluoxetine and clomipramine. These medications are used alongside reward-based desensitization and counter-conditioning. ASPCA's separation-anxiety guidance separates mild cases from moderate or severe ones. Mild cases respond to a stuffed food puzzle and a careful staged-departure plan. For moderate or severe cases, ASPCA recommends a structured behavior-modification program. ASPCA also notes that medication can be very helpful, especially in severe cases. AVSAB's humane-training position statement adds the welfare guardrail: aversive methods should not be used. That includes shock and electronic collars, prong collars, choke chains, and leash corrections.

The recommended order of operations comes from the Merck Veterinary Manual, ASPCA, and Karen Pryor Academy:

- Rule out medical contributors with a veterinary check.
- Capture baseline video to distinguish boredom from confinement anxiety from separation panic.
- Suspend panic-producing absences where possible while you build a plan.
- Use reward-based desensitization and counter-conditioning to keep the dog under threshold.
- Pair supportive products to that plan.
- Discuss medication with a veterinarian or board-certified veterinary behaviorist when the dog cannot stay under threshold long enough to learn.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I need a behaviorist or veterinary behaviorist?**
A: For mild cases, a qualified reward-based trainer with explicit separation-anxiety experience may be enough. Karen Pryor Academy graduates and Certified Separation Anxiety Trainers (CSATs) are reasonable starting points. For moderate or severe cases, suspected medication needs, self-injury, or comorbid fear or aggression issues, prioritize a veterinarian and a board-certified veterinary behaviorist (Diplomate of the American College of Veterinary Behaviorists, or "DACVB") or a Certified Applied Animal Behaviorist. AVSAB's position statements and ASPCA's separation-anxiety guidance both treat severe cases as a veterinary issue, not a product issue.

**Q: Are calming supplements actually proven for dog separation anxiety?**
A: The honest answer is "evidence varies, and not in the way most marketing implies." The Merck Veterinary Manual frames calming-aid evidence as ingredient-specific and dose-specific. Some over-the-counter products may provide situational support for some dogs. They should not be presented as proven cures for clinical separation anxiety. They are not equivalent to FDA-approved anxiolytics like fluoxetine or clomipramine. For severe distress, the AVSAB and ASPCA framing is clear: ask a veterinarian about behavior modification and medication options rather than escalating supplement counts.

**Q: How do I know if my dog has clinical separation anxiety versus boredom?**
A: Video is the most useful single tool. The Merck Veterinary Manual specifically recommends video monitoring to diagnose absent-owner behavior problems. That is why the Furbo 360° Dog Camera earns its place on this list. Clinical concern rises when the dog panics specifically during departure cues or owner absence: pacing, drooling, panting at rest, vocalization, scratching at exits, escape attempts, elimination, refusal of food, or self-injury. Boredom looks different. It is usually slower to start, often expresses as destructive chewing of accessible objects, and the dog typically eats and rests during parts of the absence.

**Q: Can a pet camera make separation anxiety worse?**
A: It can, if voice audio or treat tossing startles or excites the dog. Karen Pryor Academy and AVSAB both warn that remote stimulation can increase arousal in some dogs. Use the Furbo 360° Dog Camera first for quiet observation. Add voice or treats only after conditioning while you are home. Stop using those features if your dog reacts with arousal or agitation.

**Q: Should I leave a KONG every time I go out?**
A: Only if your dog still eats when you leave and stays under threshold for the duration of the absence. ASPCA's mild-case separation-anxiety guidance specifically uses food-stuffed puzzles like the KONG Classic Stuffable Dog Toy as a counter-conditioning tool. The same guidance is explicit that for moderate or severe cases, food toys alone are insufficient. If your dog refuses the KONG when you leave, that is diagnostic information, not a product failure. Track it in your camera log and bring it to the vet.

**Q: Is medication a last resort?**
A: No — and treating it as a last resort can extend a welfare problem unnecessarily. The Merck Veterinary Manual lists FDA-approved fluoxetine and clomipramine formulations as part of canine separation-anxiety treatment, used alongside behavior modification. ASPCA's guidance specifically notes that medication can be very helpful, especially in severe cases. Medication is not a "give up on training" move. It is a tool that helps the dog stay under threshold long enough to learn. Discuss it openly with your veterinarian or a board-certified veterinary behaviorist if your dog cannot stay calm during even very short staged absences.
