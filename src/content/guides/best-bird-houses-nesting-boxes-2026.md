whenNotToBuy: |
  Do not buy a box without matching the entry-hole diameter to the species you want. This is the single most important decision in the whole category, because the hole size determines which birds can use the box. Roughly 1.5 inches suits bluebirds; about 1.25 inches suits many wrens and chickadees; and a hole too large lets in bigger, aggressive species that will take over or destroy the nest of the bird you were hoping for. ## Match the Hole to the Bird

The entry-hole diameter is the most important spec on any nest box, and it is not a rough guideline — it is a precise filter. A hole sized to about 1.5 inches admits a bluebird while keeping larger birds out. A hole around 1.25 inches suits many wrens and chickadees. And a hole even a little too big undoes the whole purpose: it lets in bigger, aggressive species that commandeer or destroy the nest. Cornell's NestWatch program publishes hole sizes species by species, precisely because this one number decides who can move in. That is why our score weights correct sizing above everything else.

This is why "attracts all birds" is a warning sign rather than a selling point on a birdhouse listing. A box built for a specific bird is a box that works; a box built to look nice and attract "birds" in general is a box that often attracts the wrong ones. Decide which species your region and yard can realistically host, look up its published hole size, and then confirm a [nesting box](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=cedar+bluebird+nesting+box&tag=petpalhq08-20) listing's own dimensions match before you buy. A dove is a special case — doves nest on open platforms rather than in cavities, so for them the right structure is an open shelf, not a small-holed box at all.

## The Features That Make a Box Functional

Beyond the hole, a working nest box needs four unglamorous things, and most decorative houses skip at least one. Ventilation gaps near the top let heat escape so a nest does not cook on a hot afternoon. Drainage holes in the floor let rain escape so a brood is not chilled or drowned in a puddle. A cleanout door — a hinged side or front — lets you clear the old nest between broods. This matters because parasites and packed-down material build up fast and harm the next clutch. And the box should have no exterior perch. Cavity nesters do not need one, and a perch only gives predators and competitors a foothold.

Cedar ties these together as the material of choice. It naturally resists rot and insects without paint or chemical treatment, which matters twice over: it lasts for years outdoors, and it avoids the treated or painted surfaces that can be unsafe next to a nest. When you are comparing boxes, look past the styling for these five signals — right hole, ventilation, drainage, cleanout, no perch, in durable cedar — and you will separate the real nest boxes from the yard ornaments. ## Placing and Maintaining Your Nest Box

Even a perfect box fails in the wrong spot, so placement is half the job. Mount the box at the correct height and orientation for your target species — NestWatch publishes these too — generally facing away from prevailing wind and driving rain and out of the harshest all-day sun. Put it on a smooth pole rather than a tree when you can, and add a predator baffle below it. Climbing predators like raccoons, cats, and snakes are the leading cause of nest failure, and a baffle on the pole is the most effective single defense you can add. A box on an unbaffled post is an easy target, no matter how good the box itself is.

Maintenance is the other ongoing part, and it is light but real. Clean the box out between broods and again at the end of the season through the cleanout door, removing the old nest and any debris so the next occupants start fresh and parasite-free. Check the box before each nesting season for damage, secure mounting, and a clear drainage and ventilation path. ## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How do I match the entry-hole size to the bird I want?**

A: Look up the published hole size for your target species — Cornell's NestWatch program lists them — and confirm the box's listed hole diameter matches before you buy. Roughly 1.5 inches suits bluebirds, and about 1.25 inches suits many wrens and chickadees, while a hole even slightly too large lets in bigger, aggressive birds that evict smaller songbirds. The hole is the single most important spec on the box, so treat a vague "attracts all birds" claim with suspicion and choose a box built and sized for a specific species.

**Q: Why should a nest box not have a perch?**

A: Because cavity-nesting songbirds do not need one to get in, and a perch only helps the wrong visitors. An exterior perch gives predators and aggressive competitor birds a foothold to reach into or harass the nest, so the best nest boxes deliberately leave it off. A perch on a birdhouse is usually a sign it was designed to look charming rather than to function. If a box you like has one, remove it if you can, and if you cannot, choose a different box.

**Q: Where and how high should I mount a nest box?**

A: Follow the published guidance for your species on height and orientation, generally facing the entrance away from prevailing wind and driving rain and out of harsh all-day sun. Mount it on a smooth pole rather than a tree trunk when possible, and add a predator baffle below the box, since climbing predators are the top cause of nest failure and a baffle is the most effective defense you can add. **Q: How do I clean a nest box between broods?**

A: Use the cleanout door — a hinged side or front — to open the box and remove the old nest and any debris after a brood has fledged, then again at the end of the season. Clearing old material keeps parasites and packed-down nesting matter from harming the next clutch, which is exactly why a box you cannot open is a poor choice. A box without cleanout access accumulates problems over time, so the ability to open and empty it is a core feature, not a nicety.

**Q: Is cedar really better than painted or treated wood?**

A: For a nest box, yes. Cedar naturally resists rot and insects without paint or chemical treatment, so it lasts for years outdoors and avoids putting treated or painted surfaces next to a nest, where chemicals could be a concern. Painted and pressure-treated woods may look nice or promise durability, but untreated cedar gives you the weather resistance you want without that trade-off. If you do choose a finished box, keep any paint or sealant to the exterior only and never inside the cavity or around the entrance.
