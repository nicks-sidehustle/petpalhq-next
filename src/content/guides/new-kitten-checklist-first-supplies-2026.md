## Get the litter box right first

The litter box is the first thing to set up, because a kitten that avoids its box turns into a house-training problem in days. Two rules matter more than the box you choose. First, put out one more box than you have cats — one cat means two boxes — so the kitten always has a clean, available option. Second, keep them scooped, because cats are fussy about a dirty tray. The covered Nature's Miracle box here suits kittens that like privacy, but the hood is a genuine preference split, so watch the first weeks and switch to an open pan if your kitten balks. If you would rather automate the scooping altogether, the [automatic litter box](/guides/best-automatic-litter-boxes-2026) roundup weighs the self-cleaning options and what they cost to run.

## Redirect the scratching instinct early

Scratching is not a behavior problem to correct — it is an instinct a cat uses to condition its claws and mark territory, and a kitten will scratch something whether or not you provide a target. The trick is to give it a better one before it settles on your couch. A tall, stable post wrapped in rough sisal, placed where the family spends time, wins far more often than a post hidden in a spare room. Start the kitten on it early, reward it for using it, and the furniture stops being interesting. The Purrlato tower is a fine first piece, but a large or athletic cat will eventually want more height — the [cat tree](/guides/best-cat-trees-large-cats-2026) roundup covers taller, sturdier builds for when a kitten grows into a serious climber. For wand toys, puzzle feeders, and scratchers to pair with the tower, the [enrichment toys and scratchers](/guides/best-cat-enrichment-toys-scratchers-2026) guide has the full range.

## Water, whiskers, and comfortable meals

Cats evolved from desert animals and are chronically bad at drinking enough, so making water appealing pays off for a kitten's long-term urinary health. A fountain helps because many cats drink more from moving water than from a still bowl; if you are comparing models, the [cat water fountain](/guides/best-cat-water-fountains-2026) roundup covers quiet pumps and easy-clean designs. The bowls matter too. A deep, narrow dish presses on a kitten's whiskers with every bite, so a wide, shallow one keeps meals calm — you can find plenty of [shallow ceramic cat bowls](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=shallow+ceramic+whisker+friendly+cat+bowls&tag=petpalhq08-20) in this shape. Keep the food and water a little apart, since many cats prefer not to drink right next to where they eat.

## Vet visits and burning kitten energy

Two more essentials round out the first weeks, and both are about routine rather than gear. The early months mean several vet trips for the kitten vaccination series, so a carrier the kitten already knows makes every visit calmer. Leave it open at home with a blanket and the odd treat inside, so it reads as a familiar bed rather than the box that only appears before a frightening car ride. When your kitten outgrows a soft starter carrier, the [cat carrier and backpack](/guides/best-cat-backpack-carriers-2026) roundup covers sturdier and travel-ready options. The second essential is daily play: ten minutes of wand chasing before bed burns off the evening zoomies and teaches a kitten that fingers are not prey, which spares your hands the ambush games kittens otherwise invent. ## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Should I get a hooded litter box or an open one for a kitten?**

A: It depends on the individual cat, and you may not know until you try. A hood, like the one on the Nature's Miracle box, gives privacy and contains litter and odor, which many cats and their owners prefer. But some cats feel trapped in an enclosed box and will avoid it, sometimes eliminating just outside instead. Start with whichever you like, watch the first two weeks closely, and switch formats at the first sign of avoidance rather than assuming the kitten is misbehaving.

**Q: How many litter boxes does one kitten need?**

A: The standard rule is one box per cat plus one extra, so a single kitten should have two. It sounds like a lot for one small animal, but a spare box means there is always a clean option, and it matters even more once the kitten is grown or if you add a second cat later. Place the boxes in different quiet spots rather than side by side, and keep them away from the food and water so the kitten does not have to eat and eliminate in the same corner.

**Q: Why does whisker fatigue matter for a kitten's bowl?**

A: A cat's whiskers are packed with nerves and sensitive to touch. A deep, narrow bowl brushes those whiskers against its sides at every bite, which some cats find unpleasant enough to paw food onto the floor or leave food behind. A wide, shallow dish like the FlaggeZG bowls lets a kitten eat without that contact. It is not a medical emergency, but if your kitten is flinging food or eating oddly, the bowl shape is a cheap thing to rule out before you worry about the food itself.

**Q: My kitten won't drink from a bowl — will a fountain fix it?**

A: Often it helps, because moving water draws many cats in where still water does not, so a fountain like the ORSDA is worth trying for a kitten that ignores its bowl. But not every cat prefers a fountain, and some are startled by the pump noise at first, so give it a week near the food before you judge. If the kitten still drinks very little, or seems lethargic, that is a vet question — persistent low water intake can point to a health issue, not just a preference.

**Q: What should I NOT buy for a new kitten yet?**

A: Skip the expensive and the premature. You do not need a self-cleaning robot litter box, a cat wheel, a designer condo, or a wardrobe of outfits in the first weeks. You also should not grab flea or worming products off a shelf — those come from your vet, matched to the kitten's age and weight. Buy the six basics, see what your kitten actually gravitates to, and let its habits tell you what to add. Most of the aisle is for you, not the cat.
