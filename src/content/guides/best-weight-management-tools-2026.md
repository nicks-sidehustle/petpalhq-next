whenNotToBuy: >
  Skip every tool in this guide and call your veterinarian first if your pet has
  lost weight unintentionally, is showing appetite changes alongside other signs
  (vomiting, lethargy, increased thirst), or is severely obese — the AAHA Weight
  Management Guidelines and the Merck Veterinary Manual both treat severe
  obesity, sudden weight changes, and concurrent disease as conditions requiring
  a veterinary workup before any owner-driven plan, since underlying endocrine
  disease (hypothyroidism, Cushing's), orthopedic limitation, or chronic illness
  can mimic or cause weight problems and respond differently than simple caloric
  excess. Skip "fat-burner" or "metabolism booster" supplements entirely; the
  Association for Pet Obesity Prevention and Tufts Petfoodology both treat these
  as poor-evidence territory, and the Merck Veterinary Manual's weight-loss
  framework relies on caloric restriction and structured monitoring, not
  metabolic shortcuts. Skip severe calorie restriction without veterinary
  oversight; the Merck Veterinary Manual's roughly 1% body-weight-per-week
  target is the safer ceiling, and crash dieting in cats specifically can
  trigger hepatic lipidosis. And skip a retail weight-loss food alone if the
  household has not yet committed to measured portions and a treat budget;
  APOP's 2022 report found owners consistently underestimate the calories they

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How common is pet overweight or obesity?**
A: The Association for Pet Obesity Prevention's 2022 clinic-based U.S. survey found 59% of dogs and 61% of cats assessed by veterinary professionals were overweight or had obesity. The Merck Veterinary Manual notes obesity is one of the most common companion-animal health problems, and the AAHA Weight Management Guidelines treat it as a chronic condition requiring structured response, not a cosmetic concern.

**Q: How fast should a pet lose weight?**
A: The Merck Veterinary Manual frames roughly 1% of body weight per week as a reasonable target, with monitoring about every two weeks and program adjustments based on response. For a 70 lb dog, that is about 0.7 lb per week; for a 12 lb cat, it is about 0.12 lb per week. Crash dieting is dangerous — the AAFP and Cornell Feline Health Center both flag rapid weight loss in cats as a hepatic lipidosis risk factor.

**Q: Is a scoop enough, or do I really need a kitchen scale?**
A: A scoop is better than eyeballing, but Tufts Petfoodology has been explicit that volume measurement is one of the largest sources of overfeeding error — kibble densities vary across brands, scoops vary across owners, and cup-based feeding routinely delivers more calories than the manufacturer's labeling intends. Most weight-loss food packaging already prints kcal/gram, so a $14 kitchen scale like the Etekcity Food Kitchen Scale, Digital Grams and Ounces for Weight Loss is the cheapest fix in this category.

**Q: Should I use a Body Condition Score chart at home?**
A: Yes. AAHA's Weight Management Guidelines and WSAVA's BCS chart are designed for this — both are free PDF downloads from the respective organizations, and AAHA frames body-condition scoring as a core assessment tool that complements weight on the scale. Print one, post it on the fridge alongside the scale, and use it monthly. A scale tells you whether the trend is working; a BCS chart tells you whether the destination is right.

**Q: Are weight-control foods enough by themselves?**
A: No. The AAHA Weight Management Guidelines, the Association for Pet Obesity Prevention's 2022 report, and Tufts Petfoodology all keep landing on the same point — caloric restriction works, but the formula is one piece. Treats, table food, and unmeasured snacks are where the plan fails. Switching to Hill's Science Diet Adult Perfect Weight Dry Dog Food, Chicken & Brown Rice, 25 lb. Bag while continuing to free-feed snacks is the most common defeat pattern in this category.

**Q: When should I see a veterinarian instead of buying tools?**
A: When the problem is unintentional weight loss, sudden appetite change, severe obesity, or weight issues alongside other signs (lethargy, increased thirst, vomiting). The AAHA Weight Management Guidelines and the Merck Veterinary Manual both treat severe obesity, sudden weight changes, and concurrent disease as conditions requiring a veterinary workup before any owner-driven plan — endocrine disease (hypothyroidism, Cushing's), orthopedic limitation, and chronic illness can all mimic or cause weight problems and respond differently than simple caloric excess. ROYAL CANIN Canine Satiety Support Dry (26.4 lb) and other prescription-grade therapeutic diets are part of those conversations, not consumer purchases ahead of them.
