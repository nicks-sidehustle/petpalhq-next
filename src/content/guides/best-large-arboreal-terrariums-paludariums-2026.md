Large arboreal enclosures are where reptile keeping stops being a shelf tank and becomes a room feature. Tree-climbing species — crested geckos, gargoyle geckos, veiled and panther chameleons, dart and tree frogs — need height far more than floor space, and they need front-opening access so feeding does not turn into a top-down chase. The catch is that the two things these animals need most, humidity and airflow, pull in opposite directions, and the enclosure material is what decides which one you get. Getting that choice right is the whole game.

The category sorts into two materials and one hybrid. Glass terrariums like the REPTIZOO 45 gallon hold humidity for geckos and frogs and give clear viewing, at the cost of weight. Screen habitats like the Zoo Med ReptiBreeze maximize airflow for chameleons but drain moisture fast. The paludarium — the REPTIZOO 67 gallon with its sealed water base — is the hybrid that adds a genuine water feature for frog vivaria and semi-aquatic setups. We read eight expert sources for this guide, leaning on ReptiFiles' species care sheets for the size minimums and the glass-versus-screen call, and on ARAV and Merck reptile-husbandry framing for why stagnant air harms chameleons.

This guide is for keepers who already know their species and want a large enclosure that clears its published minimum. It is not a substitute for a full care sheet, and it does not cover lighting, heat, or substrate — none of these enclosures include them. What it does is match four genuinely large, genuinely buyable homes to the arboreal animals they fit, and flag the one mistake that matters most: putting the wrong material around the wrong species.

## How to match the enclosure to the species

Start with the published minimum, not the marketing gallon count. ReptiFiles sets the single crested-gecko minimum at 18x18x24 and the single veiled-chameleon minimum at 24x24x48, and both are height-first because these animals climb. Every pick here clears the crested-gecko minimum; only the Zoo Med ReptiBreeze XL and the two 36-inch towers give a chameleon the height it needs, and only the ReptiBreeze meets the chameleon minimum on all three axes. If you keep a chameleon, that number rules out the shorter glass boxes regardless of price.

Then choose the material by the animal's humidity need. This is the decision that separates a healthy setup from a chronic problem, so it gets its own section below. In short: crested geckos, gargoyle geckos, dart frogs, and tree frogs want the moisture that glass holds, while chameleons want the airflow that screen provides. The REPTIZOO paludarium and the two REPTIZOO/PROLEE glass towers serve the first group; the Zoo Med ReptiBreeze serves the second.

Finally, decide whether you need standing water. A crested gecko does not — a humid glass terrarium with a water dish is plenty. A dart-frog or tree-frog vivarium, or a semi-aquatic build, does, and that is what the REPTIZOO 67 gallon paludarium's sealed 10-inch base is for. ReptiFiles' bioactive-build reference treats a tall enclosure with a sealed base and drainage layer as the standard foundation for a planted bioactive paludarium, and a plain terrarium cannot hold that water without a separate liner.

## Screen versus glass: the humidity and respiratory trade-off

This is the trade-off the whole category turns on, and it is more nuanced than the common "chameleons must be in screen" rule. ReptiFiles' enclosure-type overview lays it out plainly: screen enclosures maximize ventilation but drain humidity fast, while glass retains moisture and gives clear viewing at the cost of weight. Neither is universally better. The right answer depends entirely on the species and, for chameleons, on your home's climate.

For chameleons, airflow is the priority because stagnant air is dangerous. Closed glass with poor ventilation traps humid, still air, and both ReptiFiles and general ARAV husbandry guidance tie that stagnant environment to the respiratory and eye infections chameleons commonly develop. A full-screen habitat like the Zoo Med ReptiBreeze is the standard prevention. But ReptiFiles adds an important qualifier: full screen is ideal in humid climates, while dry-climate keepers should cover two sides of the cage to hold humidity and give the animal a sense of security — and in a genuinely dry home, a full-glass or PVC enclosure is actually the better call. That is why a glass tower like the PROLEE is a defensible chameleon home in a low-humidity house, and why the screen ReptiBreeze is the default everywhere humidity is easy to maintain.

For crested geckos, gargoyle geckos, and tree frogs, the priority flips. These species need sustained humidity, and glass holds it while screen sheds it. Put a crestie or a tree frog in an open screen cage and you fight evaporation all day and still lose. The REPTIZOO 45 gallon and 67 gallon are built for this group — tempered glass walls that hold moisture, with a top screen that vents just enough heat and passes UVB without opening the humidity floodgates. The lesson is simple: pick the material for the animal, then manage the downside — cover screen sides for a dry-climate chameleon, or add airflow to dense glass, rather than forcing an animal into the wrong material.

## Animal safety: heat clearance, humidity versus mold, and escape-proofing

Large arboreal enclosures concentrate three real hazards, and every one of them comes from the height and the humidity that make these enclosures worth buying.

**Heat and UV lamp clearance.** These enclosures stand 36 to 48 inches tall, so basking bulbs and UVB fixtures sit far from the animal, and heat elements or ceramic emitters can rest against or near the screen top. Arboreal species climb to the highest point available and can press against a hot fixture, causing contact burns. Always mount lamps outside the mesh or on a stand-off, run any heat source on a thermostat, and confirm the actual basking-branch temperature with a probe thermometer — never trust a bulb's rated wattage to tell you what the branch feels like. In a tall column, the difference between the top of the cage and the basking branch can be large.

**Humidity versus mold.** Glass walls hold the high humidity crested geckos, dart frogs, tree frogs, and rainforest setups need, but a sealed, densely planted paludarium with standing water grows mold and harmful bacteria if airflow is poor. Pair glass with a full screen top, run a small fan or leave a base vent gap, and use a hygrometer to keep humidity species-correct rather than perpetually saturated. Chameleons are the opposite risk: a screen habitat is chosen precisely because closed glass with stagnant air drives the respiratory and eye infections the species is prone to. Do not seal a chameleon in glass unless your climate is dry and you cover only two sides.

**Escape-proofing and glass weight.** Verify that front-opening doors latch and lock every single time — crested geckos and small frogs slip through surprisingly small gaps, and juveniles are practiced escape artists. Check that knock-down glass panels are squared so the doors close flush without a gap. And treat weight seriously: a filled paludarium with a 10-inch water base is very heavy and must sit on a rated stand or cabinet, never an unrated shelf. Match the enclosure to the species before you buy — 18x18x24 is the crested-gecko minimum, 24x24x48 is the veiled-chameleon minimum, and humidity-dependent frogs need glass with a controlled screen top, not an open screen cage.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What size terrarium does a crested gecko actually need?**

A: ReptiFiles sets the minimum for a single crested gecko at 18x18x24, and because cresties are arboreal, height is the priority — taller is better than wider. Every pick on this page clears that minimum. The REPTIZOO 45 gallon at 24x18x24 exceeds the footprint while holding the 24-inch climbing height, which makes it the value default for one crested gecko. The 36-inch REPTIZOO paludarium and PROLEE tower give even more vertical range. The number to remember is the height minimum, not the gallon count on the box, because gallons can be misleading when a tank is wide and short rather than tall.

**Q: Do chameleons need a screen cage, or is glass okay?**

A: Both can work, and the right answer depends on your climate. ReptiFiles' care sheet frames full screen as ideal in humid climates because chameleons need constant airflow — closed, stagnant glass is tied to the respiratory and eye infections the species commonly gets. That is why the Zoo Med ReptiBreeze XL, a full-screen 24x24x48 habitat, is the default chameleon pick. But in a dry climate, an open screen cage cannot hold humidity, and ReptiFiles is explicit that a full-glass or PVC enclosure is the better call there — with two sides covered for security. So screen for humid homes, glass for dry ones, and match the 24x24x48 minimum either way.

**Q: What is the difference between an arboreal terrarium and a paludarium?**

A: An arboreal terrarium is a tall, dry-floored enclosure for climbing species — humid or not, but without standing water. A paludarium adds a genuine water feature: a sealed, waterproof base that holds a pool, a drainage layer, or a semi-aquatic section alongside the land area. The REPTIZOO 67 gallon is a paludarium because its roughly 10-inch waterproof glass base can hold standing water, which a plain terrarium cannot without a separate liner. If you keep dart frogs, tree frogs, or a semi-aquatic setup, you want the paludarium base. If you keep a crested gecko, a humid terrarium with a water dish is enough.

**Q: How do I hold humidity in a tall glass arboreal terrarium?**

A: Plant it heavily, mist daily, and manage the top screen. Glass walls retain moisture well, but a full top screen vents both heat and humidity, so a densely planted enclosure with live plants and a substrate that holds water will hold levels far better than a bare tank. Use a hygrometer to check the actual reading rather than guessing, and aim for the species-correct range instead of keeping the tank perpetually soaked — constant saturation grows mold. If humidity still drops too fast, a misting or fogging system on a timer stabilizes it without daily hand-misting.

**Q: Can I keep a tree frog and a crested gecko in the same paludarium?**

A: We would not recommend it. Mixed-species cohabitation adds disease-transmission risk, competition for basking and feeding spots, and the chance of one animal preying on or stressing the other, and their exact humidity and temperature needs rarely line up perfectly. A large paludarium like the REPTIZOO 67 gallon has the space, but space is not the limiting factor — the husbandry conflict is. A single-species planted build almost always produces healthier animals. If you want both, run two enclosures.

**Q: Do I need a stand under a large glass terrarium, and how heavy do they get?**

A: Yes, and the weight is the reason. A large glass enclosure is heavy empty and much heavier once you add substrate, water, and hardware — a filled paludarium with a 10-inch water base is genuinely one of the heaviest things in a typical home. It must sit on a stand or cabinet rated for the load, never an unrated shelf or a piece of furniture you are guessing about. The PROLEE 65 gallon is the only pick that includes a stand, but even that should be verified level and rated before you load it. For the others, plan and buy the stand before the enclosure arrives.
