That makes vitamin C non-negotiable, not a bonus. Oxbow Essentials Adult Guinea Pig Food is the reliable base for it.       Where it fits the setup: pellets are the dependable floor under the diet, and stabilized vitamin C is why. The vitamin C in fresh food and in plain pellets degrades over time with light, air, and age, so a fortified pellet with a stabilized form gives a steadier daily dose than hoping the vegetables cover it. The intended pattern is simple: unlimited hay as the bulk, a measured daily portion of fortified pellets as the vitamin-C base, and a daily serving of fresh vegetables — bell peppers and leafy greens are common vitamin-C-rich choices — layered on top. A guinea pig setup goes wrong, in this guide's editorial judgment, for one reason more than any other: the animals are bought first and provisioned second. A family sees two small, gentle pigs, brings them home the same afternoon, and only then starts working out the cage, the food, and the vitamin C. The gear scramble that follows is stressful for the owner and worse for a prey animal dropped into an unfinished home. This page flips the order. Everything below is meant to be bought and set up before the guinea pigs arrive, so day one is calm.

Start with floor space, because it is the decision every other one depends on and the one no accessory can undo. Guinea pigs are ground animals — they roam, they popcorn, they forage across a flat surface, and they do not use height the way a hamster or a rat does. That means the size of the floor matters more than levels or features, and most pet-store cages are simply too small. Plan for a pair from the start, because guinea pigs are social herd animals that do best with a companion, and a pair needs more floor than one. Buy the space you will need in a year, not the minimum you can get away with in week one.

Then lock in the diet, which is where guinea pig welfare is truly won or lost. Two things are non-negotiable. Grass hay must be available at all times — it supplies the constant fiber a guinea pig's gut needs and the chewing that keeps its ever-growing teeth worn down. And vitamin C must come from food every single day, because guinea pigs, almost uniquely among small pets, cannot make their own. Without a daily source they develop scurvy. That is why a fortified pellet is treated here as medical, not optional, with fresh vegetables added on top. ## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I keep just one guinea pig?**
A: It is far better to keep two. Guinea pigs are social herd animals, and welfare guidance treats a pair as the default rather than a luxury. A lone guinea pig is prone to loneliness and boredom, and the only way keeping one works is if the owner can genuinely stand in for a companion with hours of interaction every day, which most people realistically cannot manage. Two pigs keep each other company, communicate in ways a human cannot replicate, and are generally calmer and more confident for it. The main trade-off is space, since a pair needs a larger floor than a single pig, but that is a reason to size the cage up, not a reason to keep one alone. If you are choosing between one pig with little daily time and a pair with the space they need, the pair is the kinder setup.

**Q: Do guinea pigs really need vitamin C every day?**
A: Yes, and it is genuinely medical rather than a nice-to-have. Guinea pigs are one of the few animals that cannot synthesize their own vitamin C, so they must take it in from food every single day. A chronic shortfall causes scurvy, which shows up as swollen or painful joints, reluctance to move, poor coat, slow healing, and a generally unwell pig. The reliable way to cover it is a guinea-pig-specific fortified pellet made with stabilized vitamin C, fed in a measured daily portion, backed up by fresh vegetables that are naturally rich in it, such as bell peppers and leafy greens. Ordinary rabbit food does not fit this need, and the vitamin C in plain food degrades over time, which is why a stabilized, fresh pellet matters. If a guinea pig ever seems unwell or stops eating, that is a veterinary question, not something to solve with more supplements at home.

**Q: How big does the cage need to be?**
A: Bigger than most pet-store cages, and bigger than most first-time owners expect. Welfare guidance points to a floor of at least 7.5 square feet for a single guinea pig and roughly 10.5 square feet for a pair, measured as continuous ground-level floor rather than added-up levels. Guinea pigs are ground animals, so ramps and lofts do not substitute for floor — the flat, usable space is what they actually roam and forage across. Many cages sold in stores are only three or four square feet, which is why picking a cage that clears the standard, or a C&C setup you can size to the pair, matters more than any single feature. Treat those numbers as a floor to clear, not a target to hit exactly. If in doubt, go larger, because no guinea pig has ever suffered from too much room, and a bigger space also makes daily cleaning and enrichment easier.

**Q: What exactly do guinea pigs eat?**
A: The diet has three parts, in order of quantity. First and most important is unlimited grass hay, such as Timothy hay, which should be available at all times and makes up the large majority of what a guinea pig eats — it supplies fiber for digestion and the constant chewing that keeps teeth worn down. Second is a measured daily portion of guinea-pig-specific fortified pellets, which are there mainly to guarantee the daily vitamin C the animal cannot make itself; pellets are a small, controlled part of the diet, not a free-feed food. Third is a daily serving of fresh vegetables, especially vitamin-C-rich ones like bell peppers and leafy greens, which add variety and extra nutrition. Fresh water rounds it out and must always be available. What to avoid is just as important: no muesli-style mixes that let a pig pick out the sugary bits, and treats kept minimal. Hay first, pellets measured, veg daily is the simple shape to remember.

**Q: Can guinea pigs use exercise wheels or balls?**
A: No — exercise wheels and exercise balls are unsafe for guinea pigs and should never be used. A guinea pig's spine is not built to arch the way a wheel or ball forces it to, and using one can cause serious back and foot injury. Their exercise needs are met on the flat instead: enough floor space in the cage, plus supervised floor time in a safely enclosed area where they can run and popcorn on level ground. Enrichment comes from tunnels, hideouts, foraging for hay, and simply having room to move and the company of another pig, not from a spinning device. This is one of the clearest lines in guinea pig care, and it is worth stating plainly because wheels and balls are still sold and marketed for small animals. If the goal is a more active, engaged pig, the answer is more space and more floor time, never a wheel.
