## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How often should I brush my cat?**
A: It depends on coat type. ASPCA Cat Grooming Tips recommends short and frequent brushing for cats that tolerate it; VCA's grooming and coat care for your cat describes daily brushing as the goal for long-haired cats and several-times-a-week brushing as the baseline for medium coats. Short-haired single-coated cats can usually be groomed once or twice a week, and many do most of their own grooming on their own. The cap on all of this is the cat's tolerance — AAFP feline-friendly handling guidelines are clear that pushing through stress signals is counterproductive.

**Q: Do cat brushes actually reduce hairballs?**
A: Yes, by reducing the volume of loose hair the cat ingests during self-grooming. Cornell Feline Health Center's "A Hairy Dilemma" and "The Danger of Hairballs" are both direct that more thorough at-home brushing reduces ingested fur and therefore hairball volume. Brushes do not treat the underlying cause when hairballs are frequent or painful — Cornell is specific that frequent vomiting, retching without producing a hairball, lethargy, or appetite loss are veterinary signs, not grooming problems.

**Q: Can I use a dog brush on my cat?**
A: Sometimes, with caution. Many slicker brushes (including the Hertzko in this guide) are explicitly cross-marketed for dogs and cats, and the Hertzko's manufacturer page lists both species. What you should not substitute is a dog deshedding tool for a cat — FURminator publishes separate cat and dog product lines, and the cat tool has the cat-appropriate edge geometry. ASPCA Cat Grooming Tips and r/cats consensus agree that dog-sized slickers and dog deshedders are the easy way to draw blood on a cat.

**Q: What if my cat will not let me brush them?**
A: Start with grooming gloves, not a brush. The American Association of Feline Practitioners' Feline-Friendly Handling Guidelines emphasize building tolerance gradually, matching pace to the cat, and stopping before the cat escalates. ASPCA's at-home grooming recommendations are consistent — short, calm sessions paired with treats, with the goal of a positive association first and a thorough groom second. HandsOn Pet Grooming Gloves are the format most behaviorists recommend for resistant cats because they let owners groom while petting.

**Q: When are mats too severe for home dematting?**
A: Whenever the mat is felted close to skin, when the cat reacts with pain, or when skin underneath is red, sore, or flaking. ASPCA Cat Grooming Tips warns directly against scissor mat removal, the Merck Veterinary Manual specifically warns that scissors can cut skin during home grooming, and the Professional Pet Groomers and Stylists Alliance treats severe mats as a job for a professional groomer or veterinarian. The honest rule of thumb: if a comb cannot pass through the mat in one or two gentle attempts, the mat is groomer territory — keep going at home and you risk lacerating the skin underneath.
