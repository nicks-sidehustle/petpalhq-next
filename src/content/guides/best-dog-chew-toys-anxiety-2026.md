whenNotToBuy: |
  Skip every product here as a treatment for clinical separation anxiety. ASPCA is explicit that a stuffed food toy works only for mild cases, because a highly anxious dog usually will not eat when its guardians are not home. If your dog panics seconds after you leave, refuses food when alone, drools heavily, vocalizes for long stretches, or tries to escape, that is a behavior disorder, not a toy problem. The fix is a structured desensitization and counter-conditioning program supervised by a veterinarian or a board-certified veterinary behaviorist, sometimes alongside prescription medication. Adding more toys to a plan that is not working delays the help the dog actually needs.

  Skip hard nylon and synthetic "chew bone" toys as anxiety tools. A 2025 study found that scented synthetic chews did not reduce undesired chewing, and veterinary sources including the Merck Veterinary Manual flag hard nylon chews as a dental-fracture risk — a dog can crack a tooth on something that does not give. These products are popular and durable, but durability is not the same as calming, and a fractured tooth is an expensive, painful outcome that none of the picks above carry.

  Skip edible long-lasting chews — bully sticks, dental chews, rawhide — if what you want is reusable gear. They are consumables, not equipment, so they vanish with each use and the cost recurs. ## The three calming mechanisms, and which one your dog needs

The reason this lineup looks varied — rubber, silicone, plush — is that each toy delivers a different self-soothing behavior, and dogs do not all calm down the same way. A dog that settles by gnawing wants the chew axis, and a stuffed, frozen KONG is the tool for that. A dog that settles by the slow, repetitive motion of licking wants a lick mat, which stretches a thin smear of food into ten or twenty minutes of work. A dog that settles by hunting wants to use its nose, and a snuffle mat turns a portion of kibble into a foraging puzzle hidden in fabric folds. Most dogs respond to more than one of these, which is why owners often end up with two or three of these toys in rotation rather than just one.

The ranking here is mechanism-aware, not a straight head-to-head. ## Animal safety: toppings, sizing, and supervision

The most important safety rule on this page is about what you put on the toy, not the toy itself. The American Kennel Club warns that some peanut butter contains xylitol, an artificial sweetener that is toxic to dogs even in small amounts, and the ASPCA Animal Poison Control Center fields thousands of xylitol calls a year. Read the ingredient label on every spread, every time, and use only xylitol-free products. Plain Greek yogurt, canned pumpkin with no added sweetener, and a verified xylitol-free peanut butter are safe choices; anything labeled sugar-free or "lite" is the category most likely to hide xylitol. If you ever suspect a dog has eaten xylitol, the ASPCA APCC hotline is 888-426-4435, and time matters.

Sizing and chewer-match are the next line of safety. A toy that is too small is a choking hazard, so match the size to the dog's weight and size up if the dog is between sizes. Match firmness to chewing style, too: the red KONG Classic and the softer West Paw Toppl are for average and moderate chewers, and a dog that destroys rubber needs the black KONG Extreme instead of either. The lick mat and the snuffle mat are not chew toys at all — they are feeding surfaces, and a dog that bites and tears at silicone or plush should be redirected to a rubber chew before it swallows pieces.

Finally, treat calorie load and supervision as part of safety. Food stuffed into a toy is part of the daily ration, not an addition to it, so pull the equivalent from the regular meal to avoid weight gain. Supervise the first several sessions with any new toy, inspect rubber and silicone for cracks or missing chunks between uses, and store plush and puzzle toys once the food is gone. And keep the framing honest: none of these toys is a treatment for clinical separation anxiety. ASPCA is explicit that food toys help only mild cases, so a dog that panics when alone needs a veterinarian or a board-certified veterinary behaviorist, not another toy.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Which toy should I buy first for an anxious or bored dog?**

Start with the KONG Classic. It is the most versatile toy on this page — it works dry as a chew, stuffed and frozen as a long settle, and lightly smeared as a quick distraction — and it is the only pick directly supported by a peer-reviewed isolation study. In that 2023 study, dogs left alone with a long-lasting chew engaged with it longest and looked least stressed compared with a plain toy or a mechanical dispenser. A stuffed, frozen KONG is the household version of that chew. If your dog responds more to licking than chewing, the LickiMat is a cheap, sensible second buy, and a snuffle mat adds the sniffing mechanism. Most owners end up with two or three of these in rotation rather than relying on one.

**Will a chew toy fix my dog's separation anxiety?**

No, and treating it that way can delay real help. The ASPCA is explicit that a stuffed food toy works only for mild cases, because a highly anxious dog usually will not eat at all when its owner is gone. If your dog panics within seconds of your leaving, refuses food when alone, drools, vocalizes for long stretches, or tries to escape, that is clinical separation anxiety — a behavior disorder that needs a structured desensitization and counter-conditioning program supervised by a veterinarian or a board-certified veterinary behaviorist, sometimes alongside prescription medication. A KONG or a lick mat is a routine-support and boredom tool. If your dog ignores it the moment you leave, write that down and bring it to your vet, because it is useful diagnostic information rather than a product failure.

**Are lick mats and snuffle mats safe to leave with a dog unsupervised?**

No. Both are feeding surfaces, not chew toys, and they are meant for supervised use. The American Kennel Club frames lick mats as a calming aid you use during a stress event like fireworks or a thunderstorm while you are present, not as something you leave behind when you go out. A dog left alone with a lick mat or a snuffle mat can switch from licking or sniffing to biting and tearing at the silicone or plush, and swallowed pieces are a choking and blockage risk. For genuinely unattended time, a properly sized rubber KONG matched to the dog's chewing strength is the safer choice — and even then, you inspect it for damage between uses.

**What can I safely put inside these toys?**

Stick to dog-safe, xylitol-free foods, and read every label. Plain Greek yogurt, canned pumpkin with no added sugar, broth-soaked kibble, wet dog food, and a verified xylitol-free peanut butter are all safe fillings. The single rule that matters most comes from the AKC: some peanut butter contains xylitol, an artificial sweetener that is toxic to dogs, so anything labeled sugar-free or "lite" is the highest-risk category to avoid. Freezing a stuffed KONG or Toppl makes the food last longer and turns a five-minute snack into a long settle. Keep the snuffle mat to dry kibble and treats, since wet toppings will not rinse out of plush. And remember the calories count toward the daily total — pull the equivalent from the regular meal.

**How is a snuffle mat different from a chew toy, and does my dog need both?**

A snuffle mat targets a completely different calming mechanism. A chew toy like the KONG works the jaw and satisfies the urge to gnaw, while a snuffle mat hides food in fabric folds so the dog has to use its nose to forage for it. The ASPCA describes sniffing as both stimulating and calming, and the 2023 isolation research suggests that letting a dog perform a natural foraging behavior lowers stress during time alone. Whether your dog needs both comes down to how it self-soothes: a heavy chewer may get more from the KONG, a scent-driven dog may settle faster with the snuffle mat, and many dogs benefit from rotating between mechanisms so no single toy goes stale. The snuffle mat is a complement to the chew and lick picks, not a replacement for either.

**Is the West Paw Toppl just a more expensive KONG?**

Not quite — it solves a different practical problem. Both are freezable food puzzles, and both are supported by the same isolation research on long-engagement enrichment, but the shapes diverge. The KONG is a deep cone that is ideal for frozen plugs and for an average-to-power chewer, while the Toppl is a wide, open-top Zogoflex cup that is far easier to fill with wet food and to scrape clean. The Toppl also has a stronger material set — no BPA, latex, phthalates, or silicone — and a Love It Guarantee. The catch is that it is built for moderate chewers only and costs more, so a power chewer should stay with a KONG. Buy the Toppl as a complement for easy fill-and-freeze meals, not as a tougher KONG, because it is the softer of the two.
