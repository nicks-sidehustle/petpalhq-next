## Sleeping bags vs. travel beds vs. cots

Three different form factors serve three different camping scenarios, and buying the wrong one for your use case is the most common mistake in this category.

A **sleeping bag** (Ruffwear Highlands) is the right answer for dogs camping in temperatures below 55°F, dogs packing in to a backcountry campsite, and dogs that sleep curled rather than sprawled. The collar-style opening captures body heat around the dog's neck and chest the same way a human sleeping bag captures it around yours. On a cold night at elevation, the sleeping bag is doing actual work; the travel bed is doing what a folded blanket does. The tradeoff: a dog that sleeps sprawled flat finds the collar opening confining, and the insulation that makes the bag effective in cold conditions makes it hot in warm conditions.

A **flat travel bed** (Ruffwear Dirtbag, Kurgo Loft Wander) is the answer for dogs that sleep sprawled, dogs camping in warmer conditions, and dogs at a car-camping basecamp where portability is measured in trunk space rather than ounces. A well-constructed travel bed with waterproof exterior and a washable cover is a legitimate upgrade over a dog blanket — the fabric quality, the non-slip base, and the clean path matter across a full camping season. The tradeoff: no insulation collar, no elevation, and the cold-ground conduction problem is the dog's problem, not the bed's.

An **elevated cot** (Helinox, K&H) is the answer for cold-ground conduction, joint-issue dogs, rocky campsites, and multi-night trips where accumulated cold-surface exposure becomes a problem. The elevation removes ground contact entirely — which is the physics solution that a sleeping pad only partially achieves. The tradeoff: packed size. The K&H is a car-camping product; the Helinox is the only elevated cot with a genuinely packable form factor for backpacking.

## Pack-down weight is the trail-test most owners skip

The sleeping bag that compresses to 3L is a different product from the sleeping bag that compresses to 10L, even if the warmth rating is identical. For a dog that is backpacking with a saddlebag or carried in a pack, the packed volume is the constraint that determines whether the bed fits in the kit at all. The Ruffwear Highlands earns its score partly from ~3L packed volume — it can ride in a dog's saddlebag without consuming the entire gear allocation for the trip.

The same logic applies to cots. The Helinox Elevated Dog Cot packs into a carry bag roughly the size of a large Nalgene. Every other elevated pet cot on the market packs into a bag the size of a rolled sleeping bag, which is fine in a car trunk and a problem in a backpack. The Helinox's packed size is the single feature that makes it the correct choice for dogs hiking to the campsite rather than driving.

For car-camping, the weight and packed-size constraints relax and the value calculation shifts. The K&H Bolster Cot provides a 42" x 30" sleeping surface at $54, which the Helinox does not match at any price. If you are driving to the campsite, the K&H wins the value case. If you are hiking in, the Helinox wins the form-factor case. The two products serve different trip types, and buying the Helinox for car-camping is paying a backpacking premium for a car-camping problem.

## Camp temperature management

The Ruffwear Highlands sleeping bag is a three-season product in the same practical sense that most human three-season bags are. Adequate for high desert nights in shoulder season, mountain evenings in summer, and conditions down to roughly 35–40°F with an appropriately sized dog. Not an arctic kit. The lower-limit performance depends on the dog's size relative to the bag size — a bag sized for a Lab wrapped around a Beagle will not insulate as well as a properly fitted bag, because the excess air space inside the collar adds heat loss rather than insulating against it.

The cold-ground conduction problem is distinct from the ambient-temperature problem, and the two are often conflated. A dog sleeping on the ground in a sleeping bag is still losing heat through the ground contact surface — sleeping bags do not insulate the bottom effectively because body weight compresses the fill. A sleeping pad under the sleeping bag solves this. An elevated cot solves it completely. For multi-night camping in cold conditions, the combination of a sleeping bag and an elevated sleeping surface is the correct thermal solution; neither alone is as effective as both together.

Insulated sleeping pads (not reviewed here) add R-value between the dog and the ground and are worth considering for dogs camping in conditions below 45°F without an elevated cot. The Ruffwear Highlands Pad was on the initial lookup list for this guide; it returned at $49.99, which is below the $50 floor this guide holds, so it is not reviewed here — but it is the obvious companion product to the Highlands sleeping bag for owners who want both solutions.

## Why elevated cots beat the ground for some dogs

Joint-issue dogs — those with hip dysplasia, arthritis, early-onset orthopedic problems, or any condition where hard-surface sleeping produces stiffness — benefit from elevation in the same way that human orthopedic sleepers benefit from a supportive mattress rather than the floor. The mesh surface of an elevated cot distributes weight across the entire surface rather than concentrating it at pressure points; the suspension absorbs minor movement without transmitting it through a rigid surface; and the elevation keeps the dog off whatever the ground is doing — damp, cold, rocky, uneven.

r/CampingDogs threads on camping with older dogs and dogs with joint conditions converge on elevated cots as the first upgrade for any dog that wakes stiff on a camping trip. The observation is consistent across breeds and conditions: a dog that arrives at the campsite moving well and wakes moving poorly has a sleeping surface problem. An elevated cot is frequently the intervention that resolves it.

The tradeoff for cots and older dogs: balance. Dogs with vestibular conditions, neurological issues affecting proprioception, or any balance impairment that makes them uncertain on uneven surfaces should be assessed carefully before introducing a cot. The mesh surface flexes underfoot in a way that confident dogs handle without issue but that can unsettle dogs without good balance. Watch the dog's first few minutes on a cot before leaving them unattended on one.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Is a sleeping bag really necessary, or is a travel blanket fine?**
A: It depends on the temperature. A dog sleeping at a campsite in 70°F conditions does not need an insulated sleeping bag; a travel blanket or a flat travel bed is adequate. A dog camping in conditions below 50°F at night — which is most mountain and high-desert camping in spring, fall, and shoulder seasons — benefits from real insulation. The Ruffwear Highlands is not the kind of product you buy for every camping trip; it is the product that makes cold-weather camping comfortable rather than a welfare question. If your camping is limited to summer evenings in mild climates, start with the Kurgo Loft or the K&H cot rather than a sleeping bag.

**Q: My dog has hip dysplasia. Which product do I prioritize?**
A: Elevation first. Cold-ground conduction accelerates joint stiffness in dogs with existing orthopedic conditions, and the elevated cot addresses the physics problem that sleeping bags and flat beds do not solve. The K&H Bolster Cot at rank 4 is the value-tier choice for car-camping dogs with joint issues; the Helinox is the premium choice for dogs hiking to the campsite. The bolsters on the K&H are worth noting — many dogs with joint pain prefer to press against a surface while sleeping, and the bolsters replicate that configuration without requiring the dog to sleep against a tree root. Consult your veterinarian about specific sleep surface recommendations for your dog's condition; PetPalHQ is not a substitute for orthopedic veterinary guidance.

**Q: How do I know what size sleeping bag to get?**
A: Measure the dog's length from nose to base of tail, and measure chest girth. Ruffwear publishes a size chart for the Highlands that is reliable when followed. The failure mode is ordering by dog breed rather than by actual measurement — breed standards do not account for individual size variation, and a sleeping bag that is too large insulates poorly because the excess air space inside the collar does not heat efficiently. A bag that is too small restricts the dog's natural sleeping curl and produces the same poor insulation outcome from the other direction.

**Q: Can I use a human sleeping bag for my dog?**
A: Technically yes; practically it is a poor solution. Human sleeping bags are designed for the body proportions of a human — wide at the shoulders, narrowing at the feet, with a collar-style hood at the top. A dog's body proportions are different, and a dog sleeping in a human bag slides to the foot end where the insulation is compressed and the warmth is worst. The Ruffwear Highlands is designed around dog body proportions, which is why it produces better results than a borrowed human bag at similar warmth ratings. For occasional or emergency use, a human bag works; for a dog that camps regularly, a purpose-built bag is the correct investment.

**Q: Do I need both a sleeping bag and a cot, or is one enough?**
A: For most three-season camping conditions, one is enough. A sleeping bag handles the temperature problem; a cot handles the ground-contact problem. They solve different physics problems, and for cold conditions the combination is more effective than either alone — but most dogs camping in the 40–60°F range do well with just the sleeping bag if they are on a flat, reasonably dry surface. The combination becomes worth considering for multi-night basecamp trips in shoulder-season conditions, for dogs with joint issues that make cold-ground exposure painful, and for dogs camping in consistently cold or damp environments. If you are choosing one, start with the sleeping bag (Ruffwear Highlands) for cold weather and the cot (K&H for car-camping, Helinox for backpacking) for joint-issue dogs.

**Q: Are any of these picks actually suitable for backpacking?**
A: Two of the five: the Ruffwear Highlands Sleeping Bag (~3L packed volume, lightweight enough for a dog saddlebag) and the Helinox Elevated Dog Cot (packs to roughly a large Nalgene, designed for backpacking use). The other three — Ruffwear Dirtbag, K&H Bolster Cot, and Kurgo Loft Wander — are car-camping or basecamp products. The K&H is explicitly a car-camping product; the Dirtbag and the Kurgo pack adequately for car trunks but not for backpacking kit weight budgets. If your dog is hiking in, the Highlands and the Helinox are the two products on this list worth evaluating.
