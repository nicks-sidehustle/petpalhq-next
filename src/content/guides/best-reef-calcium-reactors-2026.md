A reef calcium reactor does one quiet, demanding job. It supplies a steady stream of calcium and alkalinity so a growing reef holds stable chemistry. Inside the reactor, CO2 lowers the water's pH. That acidic water slowly dissolves calcium carbonate media. The enriched effluent then drips back into the tank, replacing what stony corals pull out to build skeleton. Done right, a reactor automates the single biggest chore on a busy reef.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What does a reef calcium reactor actually do?**

A: A calcium reactor supplies calcium and alkalinity to a reef tank automatically by lowering the water's pH with CO2 inside a sealed chamber. That acidic water dissolves calcium carbonate media, usually crushed coral or aragonite, and the enriched water, called effluent, drips back into the tank. This replaces the calcium and carbonate that stony corals consume to build their skeletons, so the reactor acts as a self-replenishing source that holds your parameters steady around the clock.

**Q: Do I need a calcium reactor, or is two-part dosing enough?**

A: It depends on your tank's demand, because a lightly stocked reef with modest calcium use is fine on [two-part dosing](/guides/best-reef-dosing-pumps-2026) or kalkwasser. Once a fast-growing SPS reef consumes a lot of alkalinity each day, hand-dosing or even a doser becomes expensive and tedious, whereas a calcium reactor scales well with high demand and runs largely hands-off. Many reefers start with two-part dosing and move to a reactor only as their corals grow and their consumption climbs.

**Q: What else do I need to run a CO2 calcium reactor?**

A: A reactor body is only part of the system, since you also need a CO2 cylinder and a CO2 regulator with a needle valve or solenoid. Most reefers add a pH controller to manage the CO2 feed automatically, and you still need calcium carbonate media to fill the chamber. Budget for the full setup rather than the reactor alone, because the cylinder, regulator, and controller together can cost as much as the reactor itself.

**Q: What pH should the effluent inside the reactor sit at?**

A: Bulk Reef Supply recommends a reactor pH setpoint between 6.3 and 6.8, and the most common target is around 6.5. A pH controller holds that setpoint by feeding CO2 only when it is needed, so a lower pH dissolves media faster but can push too much CO2 into the display tank. Always dial the effluent drip rate slowly, and test your tank's calcium and alkalinity often while you tune the reactor in.

**Q: How is a kalkwasser reactor different from a CO2 calcium reactor?**

A: They reach the same goal through different chemistry, because a CO2 reactor dissolves solid media with carbonic acid while a kalkwasser reactor dissolves calcium hydroxide powder into saturated limewater. The clear limewater from a unit like the IceCap KM-120 then feeds the tank, often through an [automatic top-off line](/guides/best-automatic-aquarium-ato-systems-2026), and kalkwasser needs no CO2 gear while helping hold a higher pH. The catch is that kalkwasser cannot match a CO2 reactor's output on a high-demand reef.

**Q: How big a calcium reactor do I need for my tank?**

A: Match the reactor's rating to your tank size and calcium demand, then size up if your reef is SPS-heavy and consuming alkalinity quickly. The Aqua Excel CR30 suits tanks up to 125 gallons and the AquaReady cTech T-1 covers up to 300 gallons. For the Reef Octopus pair, read the demand-tiered ratings rather than the headline number: the CR200 reaches up to 500 gallons at light demand but only about 300 on a heavy SPS load, and the CR220 runs one tier higher at roughly 300 to 400 gallons heavy and up to 600 light. Size by the heavy-demand figure if your reef is SPS-heavy, because the headline number assumes light consumption.
