## Space and Impulses, Not Gadgets

The whole checklist rests on one idea: a new puppy is a project in managing space and impulses, not a shopping list of gadgets. In the first months a puppy cannot yet be trusted with a whole house or with its own urges. It will pee where it stands, chew what it finds, and pull toward whatever smells interesting. The right supplies do not train the puppy for you. They set up the situation so good habits are easy and bad ones are hard.

That is why the six picks split into two groups. Three of them manage space: a crate for a safe den at night, a playpen for supervised freedom by day, and a carrier for the trips to the vet. Three manage impulses: a slow feeder for a puppy that gulps, a harness for a puppy that pulls, and a teething chew for a puppy that mouths everything. Buy along those two lines and you have covered what the first months actually demand. Almost everything else in the pet-store aisle is an extra you can add later, once you know this particular puppy.

## The Crate-and-Playpen System

Space starts at home, and the crate is the anchor. A crate gives a puppy a den to sleep in and does the heavy lifting of house-training, since a puppy avoids soiling where it rests. The trick is size: too big and the puppy just uses a far corner as a toilet. A crate with a divider solves that, and the sizing math for different breeds is worth reading in the full [heavy-duty dog crate](/guides/best-heavy-duty-dog-crates-2026) roundup before you buy.

The playpen is the daytime half of the same system. It gives a puppy room to move without handing over the whole house, which is where cords get chewed and rugs get stained. Penned in a safe square, a puppy can play and nap while you cook or work nearby. If you want to compare pen heights and footprints, the [exercise pen and playpen](/guides/best-heavy-duty-dog-exercise-pens-playpens-2026) guide lays out the options. Together the crate and pen give a puppy exactly as much space as it can handle at each stage, and not an inch more.

## Mealtime and Leash-Training Safety

Two daily routines carry a real safety angle in the first months: eating and walking. Puppies gulp food fast enough to choke or bring it back up, and a slow feeder is the plain fix — a removable insert in the bowl forces smaller bites, and a stainless bowl survives teething better than plastic. You can weigh bowl shapes and insert designs in the [anti-gulp slow feeder](/guides/best-slow-feeders-anti-gulp-bowls-2026) roundup before committing to one.

If you just want to solve the gulping today, a basic [stainless steel slow feeder bowl](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=stainless+steel+slow+feeder+dog+bowl&tag=petpalhq08-20) costs little and starts working the moment it hits the floor, no training required.

Walking is the other one. A puppy that pulls on a flat collar puts all that force on a soft, still-growing throat. A front-clip harness moves the pressure to the chest and turns the puppy back toward you instead. It does not teach loose-leash walking on its own, but it makes practicing safe. The trade-offs between clip positions and fit are covered in the [no-pull harness](/guides/best-no-pull-harnesses-leash-manners-2026) guide.

## Teething and the First Vet-Visit Run

The last two picks cover the messiest and the most nerve-wracking parts of the first months. Teething runs from roughly the day you bring the puppy home to about six months, and a teething puppy chews to soothe sore gums. A frozen chew gives that urge somewhere safe to go instead of your furniture. A wider set of chew options for different chewers and anxiety levels sits in the [dog chew toy](/guides/best-dog-chew-toys-anxiety-2026) roundup.

Keeping a cheap [freezable puppy teething toy](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=freezable+puppy+teething+toy&tag=petpalhq08-20) in the freezer means there is always a cold chew ready the moment a puppy starts gnawing on a table leg.

The vet visits are the other early reality. The first months bring a vaccination series and a checkup or two, and a squirming puppy needs a safe way to travel. A soft-sided carrier keeps the puppy secure in the car and calm in the waiting room. Sizes and airline rules vary, so the [airline-approved carrier](/guides/best-airline-approved-pet-carriers-2026) guide is worth a look if you expect to fly. For most owners, though, the carrier is simply the safest way to get a small puppy to its shots and back.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What size crate should I buy for a puppy that will grow?**

A: Buy for the adult dog, then use a divider to shrink the space to puppy size now. A crate sized for a full-grown dog is too big for a young puppy, which will just use the spare corner as a bathroom and undo your house-training. If the puppy is a mixed breed and the adult size is a guess, estimate from the parents or the paws and err on the larger side — a divider lets you dial a too-big crate down, but you cannot stretch a too-small one.

**Q: When should a puppy start wearing a harness instead of a collar?**

A: Keep a flat collar with an ID tag on from day one, but bring in the harness for any actual leash walking, once your vet clears the puppy to be out. The collar is for identification; the harness is for control without neck strain. One edge case worth stating: do not leave the harness on around the clock, because the straps can chafe a puppy and a bored puppy will chew them — take it off in the crate and between walks.

**Q: How long does puppy teething last?**

A: Roughly until six or seven months, when the adult teeth have finished coming in. The heaviest chewing tends to cluster in that window, which is why the Pupstages stick is marketed for the 0-6 month stage. If a puppy is still destroying things well past that point, the cause is usually boredom or habit rather than teething, so the fix shifts from a cold chew to more exercise, training, and mental work.

**Q: Should I introduce the playpen or the crate first?**

A: Both go in early, because they do different jobs — the crate is for sleep and nighttime from day one, and the pen is for supervised daytime hours. The mistake is using the crate as an all-day holding pen. Over-crating a puppy that cannot yet hold its bladder for long stretches backfires on house-training and on the puppy's view of the crate, so let the pen carry the daytime containment the crate should not.

**Q: What puppy supplies can I skip buying for now?**

A: Hold off on the extras that get chewed or outgrown — a plush designer bed, a wardrobe of clothes, an expensive leather collar, giant toy hauls, and automated feeders. A puppy will destroy or grow out of most of them before they earn their price. Buy a cheap, washable, chew-tolerant bed for now, add the nicer things once the teething stage passes, and let the six essentials here prove what this particular puppy actually needs first.
