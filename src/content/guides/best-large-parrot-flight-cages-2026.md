A large parrot or macaw spends most of its life in its cage, so the cage is the single most important purchase a macaw owner makes. The numbers that matter are not the headline height on the box — they are bar spacing and floor footprint. The Merck Veterinary Manual lists a macaw or large cockatoo at a minimum of 48 by 36 by 66 inches with 1.5-inch bar spacing. Avian-veterinary guidance widely attributed to the Association of Avian Veterinarians recommends a cage at least one-and-a-half to two times a bird's wingspan. VCA Animal Hospitals goes further for the biggest macaws, listing a 4-by-5-by-5-foot minimum. Those figures are the standard every pick below is measured against.

## How to Read Bar Spacing and Footprint

Bar spacing is a safety spec, not a preference. The Merck Veterinary Manual lists 0.5-inch spacing for budgies and cockatiels, 0.75 inch for African greys and Amazons, and 1.5 inch for macaws and large cockatoos. Too-narrow bars trap a macaw's beak or foot. Too-wide bars let a small bird's head through. Match the spacing to the species, every time.

Footprint matters more than height. VCA Animal Hospitals notes that tall, narrow cages are impractical, because birds need to move side to side and stretch their wings, not just climb. A 60-inch-tall cage with a 23-inch-deep floor is a small-bird flight cage, not a macaw home, no matter how tall it stands.

## Why Build Quality Is a Macaw-Specific Concern

A macaw beak generates enough force to crack hard nuts. Lighter cages, thin wire, and simple latches do not survive that. Prevue specifies 9-gauge wire and a hex-bolt assembly birds cannot unscrew on the Empire; A&E uses heavy powder-coated steel and bird-proof door locks. Marketplace cages may match the bar spacing but not publish the wire gauge — a real gap when the bird can test every weld.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What is the minimum cage size for a macaw?**
A: The Merck Veterinary Manual lists a macaw or large cockatoo at 48 by 36 by 66 inches with 1.5-inch bar spacing as a minimum. VCA Animal Hospitals lists a larger 4-by-5-by-5-foot target for big macaws. Avian-veterinary guidance widely attributed to the Association of Avian Veterinarians frames it differently: at least one-and-a-half to two times the bird's wingspan. Treat all of these as floors, not goals, and add daily out-of-cage time.

**Q: What bar spacing does a macaw need?**
A: The Merck Veterinary Manual lists 1.5 inches for macaws and large cockatoos; broader avian-care guidance puts the safe range at roughly 1 to 1.5 inches. Wider bars let a macaw's beak and feet pass safely as it climbs. Narrow bars, like the half-inch spacing on small-bird flight cages, can trap a beak or foot. This is why the Prevue F050 and the A&E Dometop, both with much narrower bars, are not macaw cages in this guide.

**Q: Is a tall flight cage good enough for a macaw?**
A: Not on height alone. A flight cage is defined by its tall, narrow shape, which suits small birds in an aviary. VCA Animal Hospitals says tall, narrow cages are impractical for large birds, which need floor width to turn and stretch their wings. Check the floor footprint against the 48-by-36-inch macaw minimum, not the height on the box.

**Q: Can two macaws share one cage?**
A: They can if the cage is built for it. The A&E Double Macaw is designed for exactly this, with a removable divider so a bonded pair can share one large space or be separated into two roughly 32-inch bays. Two large parrots crammed into a single-bird cage is not appropriate. Pairing also depends on the individual birds; not all macaws tolerate a cage-mate.

**Q: Why is a small-bird flight cage like the F050 on a macaw list?**
A: Because it is frequently mis-sold as a "large flight cage" on the strength of its 60-inch height, and a buyer comparing by height can land on it by mistake. Prevue lists it for canaries, parakeets, and finches, and its half-inch bars confirm that. We rank it last to show exactly what a cage that is too small for a macaw looks like, so you can avoid that error.

**Q: Is a cheaper marketplace cage like the Belnest safe for a macaw?**
A: It can be, with caution. The Belnest's 1.38-inch bar spacing is in the macaw range, and it is listed for macaws. The caution is that bar gauge and exact footprint live in the product listing, not a manufacturer spec page, so there is less documentation than Prevue or A&E provide. If you buy one, verify the current listing's bar spacing and floor footprint against the macaw minimums before you commit.
