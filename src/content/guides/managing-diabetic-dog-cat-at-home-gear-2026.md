## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I use my own (human) glucometer on my pet?**
A: No — it will misread. Zoetis documents that human meters "are created and calibrated for measuring the concentration of glucose in a human's blood," and "do not account for this difference and underestimate the level of glucose in the blood, producing inaccurate results." An underestimated reading on a diabetic pet is the dangerous direction to be wrong in, which is the entire reason a species-calibrated meter like the AlphaTrak 3 exists. Use one built for dog and cat blood, and let the vet act on numbers you can trust.

**Q: How often should I test?**
A: Your veterinarian sets the frequency — this guide will not put a number on it. What the vet is after is not a single reading but a glucose curve, a series of readings across the day. A curve is what shows "time and duration of insulin effects, the nadir and degree of fluctuation in blood glucose concentration" (PMC10832679). Current-year guidance has also moved away from routine in-hospital curves toward home data. AVMA News, reporting on the 2026 AAHA Diabetes Management Guidelines for Cats, notes that "routine in-hospital blood glucose curves are no longer recommended for diabetic monitoring in cats." Follow your vet's cadence, and buy enough strips to keep it.

**Q: What exactly is a glucose curve?**
A: It is a series of glucose readings taken across the day on your vet's schedule, rather than one isolated number. Plotted together, the readings show the low point (the nadir), how long the insulin is working, and how much the level swings — which one peer-reviewed study describes as "necessary to assess time and duration of insulin effects, the nadir and degree of fluctuation." A single reading cannot show any of that. Capture the series in a paper logbook or the AlphaTrak app so the shape, not just the dots, reaches your vet.

**Q: Can an ordinary owner really do the testing at home?**
A: Most can, and the evidence is specific rather than reassuring-sounding. One four-month study found that "80% (12 of 15) of the owners of diabetic cats were willing and able to perform a series of BGCs at home" (PMC10832720). A separate cohort reported that "many owners are able and willing," with "65%" home-monitoring regularly (PMC10832679). The genuinely hard parts early on are mechanical — the same study lists "producing negative pressure with the lancing device," "producing a blood drop," and "restraining the cat" — and they ease with practice. Home data is not a consolation prize, either: in that study, "in 38% of cases, treatment based on hospital curves would have been different from that based on home curves."

**Q: We have several pets — how do I keep the others out of the diabetic one's food, and does a microchip feeder time the meal to the insulin?**
A: A microchip feeder solves the theft problem but not the timing one, and the distinction matters. The SureFeed opens "only for the registered pet's implanted microchip or RFID collar tag" so "only the correct cat gets access to the food" and "in multi-cat homes this stops food theft" (Felinediabetes.com). But it is access-control, not a timed dispenser — Felinediabetes.com is explicit that "most models are not a timed feeder… it is an access-control feeder." So it protects the measured portion; it does not deliver a meal on the insulin clock. If your vet's plan needs scheduled dispensing, that is our automatic-feeder guide's territory. Your veterinarian sets the diet and timing; the feeder only keeps the right food with the right pet.

**Q: What are the signs of low blood sugar, and what do I do?**
A: This is the one emergency worth memorizing before you need it, and the first aid is Merck Animal Health's, not ours. Signs can include "Weakness, Depression, Behavioral changes, Muscle twitching, Vocalization, Anxiety, Seizures, Coma, Death." If your pet is conscious, Merck's guidance is to "rub a tablespoon of corn syrup on his or her gums," then "when your pet is able to swallow, feed him or her a usual meal and contact your veterinarian." If your pet is unconscious or having a seizure, "this is a medical emergency. Contact your veterinarian right away." Never pour the solution into the mouth — Merck warns of the inhalation risk. Always call your vet; this is first aid, not treatment.

**Q: Do I need a cat-specific logbook if I have a cat?**
A: No. A paper glucose log is species-agnostic — the columns for time, reading, insulin, food, and notes are identical whether the patient is a cat or a dog, so the "Dog" on a logbook cover does not mean a cat needs a different one. Any daily glucose logbook works, or log in the AlphaTrak app. The habit of recording the series is what has value; the format is whatever you will actually keep up.

**Q: What about a continuous glucose monitor like the FreeStyle Libre?**
A: Some vets prefer one, and it is worth naming honestly. AVMA News, reporting on the 2026 AAHA Diabetes Management Guidelines for Cats, notes that feline monitoring "may include clinical signs, continuous glucose monitoring, and at-home glucose data." A CGM is vet-directed and used off-label in pets, though — so if your veterinarian recommends it, follow their kit and their instructions. This guide does not sell or link a CGM; it is mentioned once, here, because an honest at-home-monitoring page should acknowledge it exists.
