A clear-looking aquarium can still kill fish. The Merck Veterinary Manual, the University of Florida IFAS Extension, and the Virginia Aquarium & Marine Science Center all make the same point: water quality is about what is dissolved in the water, not whether the glass looks clean. Filtration is the equipment that makes safe water possible — but only if you understand what each part is actually doing.

This guide synthesizes the consensus across veterinary, manufacturer, university-extension, and trade-retailer sources. It answers the questions every freshwater keeper eventually asks: how filtration really works, how to size a filter for your tank, what kind of media goes inside, and the maintenance routine that protects fish over the long term. PetPalHQ does not run a testing lab. Every recommendation below is editorial synthesis of expert guidance, with sources cited by name throughout.

## What does aquarium filtration actually do?

In a recirculating freshwater aquarium, filtration has three core jobs: remove suspended waste, give nitrifying bacteria a stable home, and sometimes remove specific dissolved compounds. The Merck Veterinary Manual and Aqueon's "Aquarium Filtration Basics" article both use the same three-stage model — mechanical, biological, and chemical filtration. Understanding which stage does what is the foundation for maintaining a safe tank.

### Mechanical filtration

Mechanical filtration physically traps solid debris — uneaten food, fish waste, plant fragments, dust. Sponges, foam blocks, floss, and polishing pads do this work. The Merck Veterinary Manual is explicit that mechanical filtration should come *before* biological media in the flow path. Debris that reaches biomedia first can clog it and create low-oxygen problem zones inside the filter.

### Biological filtration

Biological filtration is the load-bearing part of the system. Fish excrete ammonia; decomposing food adds more. Two groups of nitrifying bacteria convert that ammonia into nitrite and then nitrate. Aqueon's "Beneficial Bacteria In Your Aquarium" article and the Merck reference both emphasize that these bacteria live mainly on filter media and other wet surfaces, *not* floating in the water column. That is why filter media is colonized infrastructure, not a disposable cartridge.

The University of Florida IFAS Extension warns that ammonia at any non-zero level is dangerous in a settled tank. The toxic free-ammonia fraction rises sharply with pH and temperature. Nitrite is also dangerous because it disrupts oxygen transport in fish blood. Without working biological filtration, both compounds rise fast.

### Chemical filtration

Chemical filtration removes dissolved compounds that mechanical and biological media cannot. Activated carbon adsorbs odors, discoloration, tannins, and medication residues. Specialty resins like Seachem Purigen polish water further. Ion-exchange media like Seachem Zeolite can temporarily bind ammonia in emergencies. The Aqueon filtration article frames chemical filtration as situational rather than mandatory. Beginners often think a clean tank needs carbon running 24/7 — it does not.

### Why biological filtration matters more than beginners think

Aqueon's "The Nitrogen Cycle" article and the Aqueon "Starting A New Aquarium" 60-day guide both make the same warning: a freshly set-up tank with no established bacteria colony cannot process waste, no matter how expensive the filter. The Virginia Aquarium & Marine Science Center's water-quality lab content uses the same framing. Clear water is not proof of safe water.

That is the central editorial message of this hub: filters move water and house bacteria, but the bacteria themselves take weeks to establish. The University of Florida IFAS Extension notes that a new biofilter often needs 6 to 8 weeks to fully mature.

## How do you choose the right aquarium filter for your tank?

The marketing on a filter box gives you a tank-size range and a GPH (gallons-per-hour) rating. Both numbers are useful starting points, but neither is a guarantee. Real delivered flow is typically lower than the box rating once hose runs, dirty media, and vertical lift are factored in. Bioload always matters more than tank volume alone.

### Flow rate and turnover rate

**Flow rate** is the rated water movement of the pump. **Turnover rate** is flow divided by tank volume — a 200 GPH filter on a 40-gallon tank gives a nominal 5x turnover. The math is simple. The harder reality is that *real* delivered flow is usually lower than the box rating. Hoses, elbows, vertical lift, dirty media, and clogged intakes all reduce it. LiveAquaria's flow-rate guide is direct on this: head height, tubing turns, and clogged media all reduce real-world flow. Fluval's 07-series documentation distinguishes "pump output" from the lower in-system circulation rate once filtration media are installed.

A practical baseline used by reputable aquarium-education sources is roughly 4x average turnover for many community tanks. Bettas and planted tanks usually do better at the gentler 3x to 5x range, while goldfish and other heavy-bioload species benefit from 6x to 10x.

### Media capacity

Media capacity means usable internal volume for sponge, floss, ceramic biomedia, bio balls, carbon, or specialty resins. More media space generally means longer contact time, more bacterial habitat, and more flexibility in staging your mechanical, biological, and chemical media. Fluval's 07-series canister documentation emphasizes "maximized media capacity" with multiple chambers. Canisters and sumps physically hold far more media than nano internals or starter HOBs.

### Tank-size matching

Manufacturer ratings are a starting point. Fluval's "Aquarium Care Guide" advises moving up one size for heavily stocked tanks, and Aqueon's goldfish and shrimp guides repeat the same logic. Goldfish are the canonical example — their high oxygen demand and high waste output justify oversizing. Bettas and shrimp tanks need the *opposite* approach. They are stressed by aggressive flow.

### When to size up

Size up when any of the following apply: heavy stocking, messy fish (goldfish, larger cichlids, larger plecos), heavy feeding, deep substrate that traps detritus, longer hose runs on a canister, or older equipment where the impeller and chambers have lost some efficiency to wear and biofilm.

## What does each type of filter media do, and in what order should it go?

The correct order is coarse mechanical first, biological next, chemical last. That sequence catches solids early, protects biomedia from clogging, and lets carbon or Purigen polish water only after the earlier stages have done their work. Media roles, intervals, and cautions in the table below are drawn from Merck's system-design guidance. Manufacturer documentation from Fluval, Seachem, Aqueon, and Marineland informs the rest.

| Media type | Primary role | Best use | Replace / rinse guidance | Watch-outs |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Sponge / foam | Mechanical first, biological second | Everyday debris capture plus bacterial surface area | Rinse in removed tank water; replace only when deteriorated or flow can't be restored | If neglected, clogs and drops flow |
| Floss / poly pad | Fine mechanical filtration | Trapping smaller particles after coarse foam | Replace when clogged or physically degraded | Excellent for clarity, poor long-term bacterial preservation if swapped too aggressively |
| Ceramic rings | Biological filtration | Stable bacterial habitat in HOBs, canisters, sumps | Gently swish in aquarium water; replace in portions, not all at once | Overcleaning or chlorinated rinsing defeats the purpose |
| Bio balls | Biological filtration | Canisters, wet/dry systems, larger filters | Rinse gently in established aquarium water; replace only if damaged and only in part | Not a polishing medium; needs mechanical pre-filtration upstream |
| Activated carbon | Chemical filtration | Removing odors, discoloration, dissolved impurities, post-medication cleanup | Replace on a short cycle compared with bio media | Useful but not mandatory in every freshwater tank |
| Zeolite | Specialty chemical filtration | Short-term ammonia control in freshwater | Use as directed, replace or regenerate per manufacturer | Not a substitute for a mature biofilter |
| Polishing pads | Very fine mechanical filtration | Clearing haze, micro-particles, "sparkling" water | Replace when clogged or when flow drops | Great for clarity, but they clog faster in dirty tanks |
| Specialty media | Targeted chemical removal | Ammonia, nitrite, phosphate, or similar specific problems | Follow short replacement windows | Solve specific chemistry problems, not general neglect |

Fluval's "Filter Media Manual" PDF is direct: biological media (BIOMAX rings, ceramic media) should be rinsed in *aquarium* water, not tap water. Chlorinated tap water kills the bacterial colonies that media exists to host. Aqueon's media guidance and Aquarium Co-Op's media-order article both make the same warning. Replacing every cartridge in one session is one of the fastest ways to crash a biofilter.

## What filtration type works best for each kind of freshwater tank?

Filtration choice depends on the species, not just the volume. Bettas need gentle flow; goldfish need heavy filtration; shrimp need intake protection. The pairings below match filtration style to species need. They draw on Aqueon's betta, freshwater shrimp, and goldfish care guides, LiveAquaria's filter selection content, and the Merck Veterinary Manual on bioload and oxygen demand.

### Betta tanks (5–10 gal)

Bettas come from slow-water habitats and tolerate aggressive flow poorly. Aqueon's betta guide recommends gentle, well-distributed flow. The strongest fits are sponge filters, low-flow internal filters, or a small HOB with a baffle on the outflow. Avoid blasting a betta with a 5x-rated HOB on full speed — the box rating is rarely the right setting for a betta tank.

### Shrimp tanks (5–20 gal)

Shrimp need intake protection. Adult shrimp can usually swim against light HOB current, but baby shrimp and molting adults can be pulled into an unprotected intake. Aqueon's freshwater shrimp guide explicitly recommends air-driven sponge filters as the safest default, or a HOB or canister with a pre-filter sponge over the intake.

### Planted tanks (10–55 gal)

Planted tanks usually want gentler, more diffused circulation than community tanks. CO2 retention, spray-bar placement, and plant mass all change what "right flow" feels like — LiveAquaria's flow guide notes that planted-tank circulation is genuinely more variable than basic community-tank circulation. Premium HOBs and well-baffled canisters are common picks.

### Goldfish tanks (20–75+ gal)

Goldfish are messy, oxygen-hungry fish. Aqueon's goldfish guide is direct that goldfish tanks need stronger filtration than community tanks of the same size, and Fluval's care guide advises stepping up one filter size for heavily loaded aquariums. A 6x to 10x turnover target is more honest than 4x for most goldfish setups.

### Community tanks (10–75 gal)

Community tanks are where HOBs and canisters compete most directly. A premium HOB is plenty for many 10–40 gallon community tanks. By 55 gallons, a canister usually wins on media volume, aesthetic cleanliness, and long-term flexibility. Marineland, Fluval, and LiveAquaria all converge on this point.

### Larger aquariums (75–180+ gal)

At 75 gallons and up, dual filtration or a sump becomes a serious consideration. The reasons are practical, not technical: redundancy if one filter fails, easier cleaning intervals, and more room for media. Fluval's 07-series documentation positions the larger 407 model for 50–100 gallon tanks. Sumps are typically used by hobbyists who are already comfortable with plumbing and overflow design.

## What maintenance routine keeps an aquarium filter working correctly?

Filtration is only half of water quality. The other half is the husbandry routine that keeps the filter doing its job. Without routine water changes, correct media management, and consistent testing, even a well-sized filter cannot maintain safe ammonia and nitrate levels over time.

### Maintenance checklist

The schedule below is a baseline drawn from Aqueon's "Starting A New Aquarium" 60-day guide, Fluval's "Aquarium Care Guide" PDF, Aqueon's "Beneficial Bacteria In Your Aquarium" article, and the Texas A&M AgriLife Extension Service's "Aquarium Setup and Maintenance" reference. Goldfish, messy cichlids, and heavily fed tanks need tighter intervals. Mature, lightly stocked planted tanks can sometimes stay stable on gentler service if testing and fish behavior agree.

**Daily**

- Check fish behavior, heater, and filter output.
- Feed conservatively; uneaten food should not sit in the tank.
- Remove obvious leftovers or dead plant matter.

**Weekly or biweekly**

- Perform a partial water change of roughly 10–25% every 1 to 2 weeks for many freshwater tanks.
- Scrape glass and spot-clean visible algae.
- Vacuum exposed debris where appropriate.
- Test ammonia, nitrite, nitrate, and pH more often during cycling, after stocking changes, or when fish behavior changes.

**Monthly**

- Rinse mechanical media in *removed aquarium water* when flow is dropping.
- Clean intake strainers, uplift tubes, and impellers.
- Replace exhausted chemical media on its shorter cycle.
- Disturb biological media only lightly, and only as needed to restore flow.

**Every few months**

- Replace worn floss, polishing pads, or compressed foam.
- Replace biological media only in part if the manufacturer recommends periodic replacement.
- Deep-clean canister hoses or hidden plumbing on canisters and sumps.

For specific cleaning-tool picks — gravel vacuums, water changers, magnetic glass cleaners, and filter-hose brushes — see the [Best Aquarium Cleaning Tools for Better Water Quality](/guides/best-aquarium-cleaning-tools-2026) spoke article.

### Mistakes that crash filters

The most expensive filtration mistake is rarely buying the "wrong" brand. It is misunderstanding what the filter is responsible for.

- **Replacing all media at once.** Fluval's filter-media manual and Aqueon's beneficial bacteria article both warn that swapping every cartridge in the same session can dump enough beneficial bacteria to destabilize ammonia and nitrite control.
- **Rinsing biomedia in chlorinated tap water.** Manufacturers repeatedly direct users to rinse biological media in aquarium water. Tap water kills the colonies the media exists to host.
- **Underfiltering for the bioload.** Box ratings are a starting point, not a promise. Heavily stocked or messy tanks should be sized up.
- **Assuming more GPH is always better.** Bettas, shrimp, fry, and many planted layouts need *gentler* flow, not stronger.
- **Relying on filtration instead of water changes.** Aqueon's "Nitrogen Cycle" article is direct — most freshwater filtration converts ammonia to nitrate, which still has to be exported through partial water changes.
- **Overfeeding and then blaming the filter.** Aqueon's "Starting A New Aquarium" guidance flags uneaten food as a leading cause of avoidable ammonia spikes.
- **Skipping testing during cycling, after deep cleaning, or after adding fish.** The nitrogen cycle is invisible until it fails. Routine testing is the only way to confirm the biofilter is keeping up.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I need chemical filtration all the time?**
A: Not necessarily. The Merck Veterinary Manual and Aqueon's filtration article both treat mechanical and biological filtration as foundational, while chemical media (carbon, Purigen, zeolite) is situational — useful for odor, discoloration, tannins, post-medication cleanup, or specific chemistry problems. Most healthy freshwater tanks do not need carbon running continuously.

**Q: Is a canister filter always better than a hang-on-back filter?**
A: No. Canisters usually offer more media volume and customization, but HOB filters from brands like AquaClear, Marineland, and Seachem Tidal are simpler, cheaper, and plenty capable for many 10–55 gallon community tanks. "Better" depends on tank size, stock, service tolerance, and flow needs. LiveAquaria's filter selection guide makes the same point.

**Q: How often should I replace filter media?**
A: Chemical media usually changes fastest — Fluval recommends replacing carbon roughly every 4 weeks. Mechanical media (sponge, floss, polishing pads) gets replaced only when clogged or physically degraded. Biological media should usually be preserved, rinsed in tank water, and replaced in part when necessary. Fluval's filter media manual and Aqueon's media guidance both emphasize the same point: biomedia is colonized infrastructure, not a disposable cartridge.

**Q: Why did ammonia or nitrite spike after I cleaned my filter?**
A: Most likely because too much biomedia was rinsed, replaced, or scrubbed at once. Aqueon's "Beneficial Bacteria In Your Aquarium" article and the Merck Veterinary Manual both note that beneficial bacteria live primarily on filter media. Aggressive cleaning can knock the biofilter back into a partial cycle. Other causes include adding too many fish too quickly and feeding too heavily for a still-maturing biofilter.

**Q: What filter is safest for shrimp?**
A: Air-driven sponge filters are the default safest option. Aqueon's freshwater shrimp guide also recommends pre-filter sponges or intake guards on HOB or canister filters to stop shrimp from being pulled in. Hikari's sponge-filter documentation frames the same product class as mechanical, biological, and aerating in one — a practical fit for shrimp-only tanks.

**Q: Can filtration replace water changes?**
A: No. Aqueon's "Nitrogen Cycle" article and the University of Florida IFAS Extension are both explicit: typical freshwater filtration converts ammonia to nitrate, which still has to be exported through partial water changes. Filtration plus water changes is the routine — neither alone is enough.

**Q: What should a beginner test routinely?**
A: At minimum: pH, ammonia, nitrite, and nitrate. New tanks, tanks after a big cleaning, and tanks with sick fish or recent stocking changes should be tested more frequently. The Virginia Aquarium & Marine Science Center's water-quality lab content treats those four parameters as the baseline for monitoring nitrogen cycle health.

**Q: Do planted tanks need less filtration?**
A: They usually need *gentler* circulation and less disruptive surface agitation, but they still need regular maintenance, debris control, and stable filtration. LiveAquaria's flow-rate guide notes that planted-tank circulation varies more than basic community-tank circulation. CO2 retention, spray-bar placement, and plant mass all change what "right flow" feels like in practice.
