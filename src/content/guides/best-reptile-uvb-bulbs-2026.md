For most pet reptile keepers, the smartest first decision is not "5.0 or 10.0?" — it is whether the bulb format and output class match the species's natural sunlight-use pattern. The Merck Veterinary Manual's *Important Husbandry Requirements for Selected Reptiles* table, the LafeberVet UVB technical PDF (Baines, 2024), and the *Journal of Zoo and Aquarium Research* paper "How much UVB does my reptile need? Distance, fixture quality, reflector design, mesh, and bulb age all change that number.

The picks below are editorial synthesis. PetPalHQ does not run a testing lab — every product on this page was selected by reviewing manufacturer-technical documentation from Arcadia Reptile and Zoo Med Laboratories, peer-reviewed UV studies from Wageningen University & Research, the Journal of Zoo and Aquarium Research UV-Tool paper, and veterinary references from the Merck Veterinary Manual and VCA Animal Hospitals. Where expert sources disagreed (most commonly on whether mercury vapor bulbs should be a beginner default), the editorial framing follows the LafeberVet technical PDF: separate the jobs when possible, use all-in-one mercury vapor only where enclosure size and species behavior support it, and verify with a UV meter when the setup is unusually tall, screened, or shallow.

## T5 HO vs T8 vs compact vs mercury vapor — what hobbyists actually face

The strongest editorial line in the source set comes from the LafeberVet UVB technical PDF and the Arcadia Reptile lamp documentation: for most commonly kept reptiles, the easiest safe default is a reflector-equipped linear T5 HO UVB lamp paired with a separate basking bulb for daytime heat. The four bulb formats break down like this:

**Linear T5 HO** is the modern default. The LafeberVet technical PDF treats T5 HO as the most flexible UVB category for common pet reptiles, and Arcadia Reptile's ProT5 documentation publishes specific UVI-vs-distance figures that beat any generic "9 to 15 inches" rule. T5 HO bulbs require a T5 HO fixture; they cannot drop into a T8 or compact socket.

**Older T8 linear** is lower output and more diffuse than T5 HO. The LafeberVet PDF positions T8 as appropriate for shallow enclosures and lower-UV species, but explicitly notes that T8 is the older format and less flexible for taller enclosures or stronger basking species.

**Compact fluorescent UVB** creates a small coverage zone with a steep gradient. The LafeberVet technical PDF is direct that compact UVB can deliver a very high UVI right at the bulb while dropping off rapidly even a short distance laterally — useful in genuinely small enclosures, but a poor default for standard 40-gallon and larger setups.

**Mercury vapor** combines UVB, UVA, visible light, and basking heat in one bulb. The LafeberVet PDF and Arcadia's D3 / D3 EVO documentation treat this as a real option for large enclosures, but the format cannot be dimmed, cannot be placed on a heat thermostat, and runs hotter than a separate UVB-plus-basking system. Independent control of UVB and basking temperature is the trade-off.

A practical taxonomy follows. High-UV baskers (bearded dragons, tortoises, aquatic basking turtles, green iguanas) need desert-class T5 HO output or an appropriately sized mercury vapor bulb. Moderate-UV species (chameleons, leopard geckos with low-level UVB gradients) need forest-class output and a gradient, not blanket coverage. Compact UVB is reserved for small enclosures where a linear lamp will not fit.

## Mesh loss, mounting distance, and bulb age — the three things that nullify a "high-output" bulb

Three setup factors can defeat any bulb, regardless of package label. The LafeberVet UVB technical PDF and the Arcadia Reptile lamp documentation are the source set's clearest references on each.

**Mesh between the bulb and the animal.** Standard reptile-enclosure mesh tops cut UVB output before it reaches the animal. The LafeberVet PDF and Arcadia's lamp documentation both warn that screen tops can block a meaningful fraction of useful UV. A bulb labeled "12%" can deliver substantially less if mounted above a fine mesh top — and the labels do not adjust for that mesh. Either compensate with mesh-aware mounting (lamp closer to the animal than the bare-air calculation would suggest) or verify with a UV meter at the basking position.

**Mounting distance.** UV intensity falls off rapidly with distance from the lamp face. Arcadia's ProT5 documentation publishes specific UVI-vs-distance numbers, and LafeberVet's technical PDF uses the same principle: a powerful lamp can be safe at a greater distance, while a weaker lamp can become uncomfortably intense if pushed too close. Always measure from the **lamp face** to the reptile's **highest basking position** — not to the floor, and not to the bottom of the enclosure.

**Bulb age.** UVB output drops with use even when the bulb still looks bright. The LafeberVet technical PDF, the VCA Animal Hospitals lighting article, and Arcadia's Lighting Guide are all aligned on this: visible brightness is judged by your eye, but biologically useful UVB is judged by the reptile's skin chemistry, and they do not track each other. A bulb that "still looks fine" can be delivering well below the target UVI months before the human eye notices.

## Replacement schedules — Arcadia 12 months, Zoo Med varies, label the install date

The conservative editorial position from the source set: replace UVB bulbs on schedule unless a UV meter shows the lamp is still delivering the species-appropriate target range.

Arcadia Reptile's Lighting Guide treats 12 months as the default replacement interval for the D3 and D3+ T5 HO lines. Zoo Med Laboratories' UVB for Reptiles Lamp Charts PDF (2023) and the ReptiSun T5 HO instructions PDF (2024) cite a similar 12-month interval for the ReptiSun T5 HO line. Mercury vapor bulbs from Zoo Med's PowerSun UV documentation use a comparable interval, but real timing depends on hours of daily use and on observed UVI at the basking position.

For keepers without a UV meter, the LafeberVet technical PDF gives the safe default: label the install date on every UVB bulb, replace on the manufacturer's schedule, and watch for species-level signs of under-exposure (poor appetite, reluctance to bask, slow growth, shedding problems) that may signal a problem before a meter would. The case for a UV meter strengthens when the enclosure is unusually tall or screened, when running multiple enclosures makes calendar-based replacement wasteful, or when the species is sensitive to over- or under-exposure (some chameleons, some aquatic species).

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I just buy a "10.0" UVB bulb and call it done?**
A: Not safely. The LafeberVet UVB technical PDF (Baines, 2024) and Arcadia Reptile's ProT5 documentation are aligned: package labels like 5.0, 10.0, 6%, and 12% do not tell the keeper the UVI the reptile actually receives at basking height. Distance, fixture quality, reflector design, mesh, and bulb age all change the number. The Journal of Zoo and Aquarium Research UV-Tool paper (Baines et al., 2016) treats microhabitat and basking behavior as the right starting frame — match the bulb output class to the species's natural sunlight-use pattern, then verify with a UV meter when the setup is hard to predict.

**Q: Do I need a separate basking bulb if I already have a strong UVB bulb?**
A: Yes, with one exception. The LafeberVet technical PDF and the Arcadia halogen lamp documentation both note that linear T5 HO UVB lamps deliver UV but not meaningful basking heat. For T5 HO setups, pair with a separate halogen or incandescent basking bulb sized to the species and enclosure. The exception is mercury vapor: bulbs like the Zoo Med PowerSun UV combine UVB, UVA, visible light, and heat in one unit, which is the format's main advantage in large enclosures.

**Q: How often do I really need to replace a UVB bulb?**
A: Arcadia Reptile's Lighting Guide and Zoo Med Laboratories' instruction PDFs both treat 12 months as the conservative default for premium T5 HO and mercury vapor lamps. The LafeberVet UVB technical PDF and the VCA Animal Hospitals lighting article both note that UVB output falls before the bulb looks dim — visible brightness is not a replacement signal. For keepers without a UV meter, label the install date and replace on the manufacturer's schedule. For keepers with a meter, replace when delivered UVI at the basking position drops below the species-appropriate target.

**Q: Can a compact UVB bulb work for a bearded dragon?**
A: It is not the right default. The LafeberVet technical PDF is direct that compact UVB creates a small coverage zone with a steep gradient — useful in small enclosures, but a poor match for a bearded dragon enclosure where a broad basking platform and a real shaded retreat both matter. For bearded dragons, the synthesis pick is a reflector-equipped linear T5 HO desert-class lamp like the Arcadia ProT5 12% Desert or the Zoo Med ReptiSun 10.0 T5 HO, paired with a separate halogen basking bulb.

**Q: Do leopard geckos really need UVB?**
A: They should not be lit like bearded dragons, but the evidence supports low-level UVB use. The Wageningen University & Research 2020 paper confirmed that leopard geckos synthesize vitamin D3 from UVB exposure, and Arcadia Reptile's Lighting Guide cites a basking UVI of roughly 2 to 3 fading to 0 at the cool end as the appropriate gradient. Modern husbandry increasingly uses low-level UVB gradients for leopard geckos — a forest-class lamp like the Arcadia D3 39W Forest is a closer match than a desert-class 10.0, and the cool end of the enclosure should still drop to zero UVI for a real retreat.
