A leopard gecko habitat is a floor plan, not a light show: belly heat concentrated on one end, a temperature that falls away to a cool end, and three hides — warm, cool, and moist — spaced across that gradient so the animal can choose its spot hour by hour. Everything in this build follows from what a leopard gecko actually is: a crepuscular, ground-dwelling lizard that is active at dawn and dusk, spends the bright hours tucked away, and takes its warmth from a surface it lies on rather than from a bright lamp it suns beneath. Get that one idea right and the kit almost designs itself; get it wrong and no amount of expensive gear rescues the setup.

That is also why a leopard gecko's kit looks nothing like a desert dragon's. A bearded dragon is diurnal and needs strong UVB and a tall basking zone it climbs toward the light to use; a leopard gecko needs neither the height nor, in the view of many keepers, the UVB, because it warms its belly on a stone and takes its vitamin D from a dusted diet. Reading a leopard gecko as if it were a small desert dragon is the fastest way to overbuild the lighting and underbuild the thing that matters — the horizontal spread of temperature and the hiding it can trust.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Does a leopard gecko need UVB lighting?**
A: This is one of the genuine open debates in reptile keeping, and the honest answer is that it is a considered choice rather than a settled requirement. For decades, keepers have raised healthy leopard geckos with no UVB at all, relying on live insects dusted with a calcium and D3 supplement to supply the vitamin D the gecko would otherwise make under ultraviolet light — and that approach still works, which is why UVB is not treated as a mandatory pick in this setup the way heat and hides are. At the same time, a growing body of opinion holds that a low-strength UVB source offers real welfare benefits even for a crepuscular animal that never basks in strong sun, and many keepers now choose to add one. The practical snag is strength: the fixtures most readily in stock tend to be desert-strength bulbs built for sun-loving lizards, which are more than a shade-dwelling gecko needs, so a UVB choice is about finding a genuinely low-output source rather than grabbing whatever is on the shelf. The position here is that UVB is optional and debated, not essential — a decision to research for a particular gecko, made alongside, never instead of, the dusted diet that a no-UVB build depends on.

**Q: Is a heat mat or an overhead halogen better for a leopard gecko?**
A: The modern consensus has shifted toward overhead heat, and the reasoning is worth understanding rather than just following. The old default was an under-tank heat mat, on the logic that a leopard gecko takes belly heat from the ground — which is true, but a mat delivers that heat by conduction through the tank floor alone, is easy to run unregulated, and does nothing for the air or surfaces the gecko moves across. Today more keepers use an overhead halogen aimed at a flat basking stone: the lamp warms the stone, the gecko presses its belly to the warm surface, and the same fixture adds a little daytime light and warms the surrounding air, building a more natural gradient. Whichever heat source is used, two things do not change — it runs through a proportional thermostat, always, and the temperatures are read at the surface the gecko contacts rather than assumed from wattage. A workable target is a warm-side surface around 88 to 92 degrees, a cool end in the low 70s, and a night drop into the high 60s that a leopard gecko tolerates well. A mat is not forbidden, but if one is used it is thermostat-controlled and treated as a supplement to, not a replacement for, a properly measured warm surface.

**Q: Why does a leopard gecko need three hides instead of one?**
A: Because each of the three does a different job, and dropping any one of them quietly compromises the gecko. A warm hide sits over the basking surface and lets the animal feel secure while it digests; a cool hide at the far end lets it feel equally secure when it needs to shed heat; and a moist hide, packed with damp moss, gives it a humid pocket to shed in. The failure mode of a single open cave is subtle. If only the warm end has a snug hide, a gecko is forced to choose between feeling safe and being the right temperature, and it will often sit too warm because security wins — a stressor with no obvious cause. If there is no moist hide, sheds go badly, and the thin skin on the toes and around the eyes retains stuck shed that can constrict and injure. All three hides should be snug enough that the gecko touches the walls, because a cavernous hide does not give the enclosed feeling the animal is looking for, and extra clutter on top of the three only makes a nervous gecko feel safer.

**Q: What substrate is actually safe, and what about calcium sand?**
A: The safe answer is layered, and it starts more conservatively than the naturalistic photos online suggest. For a new keeper, a juvenile, or any gecko that is not confirmed healthy, the safe floor is paper towel or tile — nothing loose, nothing that can be swallowed with prey — because it removes impaction risk entirely and makes it easy to spot how the animal is eating and passing waste. A naturalistic loose or clay substrate is an advanced-tier floor for a grown, healthy gecko in a tank whose temperatures are genuinely correct, since impaction danger rises sharply when an animal ingests substrate while too cool to digest properly. Presented in that order, a diggable clay is a graduation, not a default. The one point of near-universal agreement is the warning: calcium sand is not a safe substrate, despite marketing that frames it as digestible or beneficial. A gecko will ingest it while feeding, and it is a classic impaction trap. Keep it out of the tank, start on paper towel, and treat a naturalistic floor as something a healthy adult earns.

**Q: Can two leopard geckos share a tank?**
A: No — a leopard gecko is a solitary animal, and cohousing is a genuine welfare risk rather than a matter of preference. Two males will fight, often seriously, and there is no reliable way to house them together. Females cohabited in a shared tank frequently stress one another even without open aggression, and problems compound: competition for the single warm spot and the hides, subtle bullying that a keeper may not witness, and difficulty telling which animal is eating, shedding, or losing weight. A tank sized and furnished as a proper single-gecko habitat — one warm hide, one cool hide, one moist hide, one basking surface — is built around one occupant, and adding a second animal does not simply double the load, it introduces conflict the setup was never meant to absorb. The safe and standard practice is one leopard gecko per enclosure, full stop; a keeper who wants two keeps two separate, complete setups.
