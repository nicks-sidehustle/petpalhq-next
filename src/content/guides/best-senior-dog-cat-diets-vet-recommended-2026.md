## How Senior Dog and Senior Cat Needs Differ

The biggest mistake in senior feeding is treating dogs and cats as the same aging animal. They are not, and the consensus guidance splits cleanly.

Senior dogs generally slow down and need fewer calories, which is why the AAHA 2023 Senior Care Guidelines emphasize moderated calorie density paired with adequate, high-quality protein for muscle maintenance — the profile the Hill's Science Diet 7+ targets. Senior cats run the other way: AAHA and the Cornell Feline Health Center both note that healthy older cats often need higher-protein, higher-digestibility, more-palatable diets to hold onto lean body mass, which is the case for a formula like Pro Plan Prime Plus. Conflating the two — feeding a cat a calorie-cut "senior" recipe built on the dog playbook — is exactly the error the guidelines warn against. The single most evidence-backed lever for an aging pet's comfort, across species, is keeping the animal lean, which is why weight management sits at the center of senior care.

## Kidneys, Joints, and What the Bag Can Honestly Claim

Two health worries drive most senior-diet shopping — kidneys and joints — and both are easy to oversell.

On kidneys, the over-the-counter lever is phosphorus moderation, not restriction. The Merck Veterinary Manual is explicit that aggressive phosphorus and protein restriction belongs to prescription renal diets used after a chronic kidney disease diagnosis; a controlled-phosphorus senior food plus a routine wellness panel is the consensus approach for a healthy older pet. On joints, the strongest peer-reviewed evidence is for EPA and DHA omega-3 fatty acids and for weight control, with glucosamine and chondroitin carrying weaker, more commonly cited support. Several picks list glucosamine and omega-3 directly, but the omega-3 is the part with the better science behind it. For a deeper treatment of mobility, our [weight management tools guide](/guides/best-weight-management-tools-2026) covers the lean-body-condition work that does more for aging joints than any in-food additive.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**When should I switch my dog or cat to a senior diet, and does age 7 really mean "senior"?**

Seven is a common marketing threshold, not a medical switch point. AAFCO does not recognize "senior" as a life stage, so the bag is using the word as positioning. Most healthy pets transition to a senior-appropriate diet somewhere in the 7-to-10 range depending on size and species — large dogs age faster than small ones — but the better trigger is a veterinary exam, not a birthday. If your pet is healthy and at a good weight, there is no urgency; if a wellness visit turns up early kidney, weight, or mobility changes, that conversation should guide the timing.

**Are over-the-counter senior diets enough, or does my pet need a prescription kidney (renal) diet?**

It depends entirely on diagnosis. For a healthy senior, a controlled-phosphorus over-the-counter diet plus routine bloodwork is the consensus approach. Prescription renal diets are a different category — the Merck Veterinary Manual reserves aggressive phosphorus and protein restriction for therapeutic foods used after a chronic kidney disease diagnosis. Those require a veterinary prescription and are not the over-the-counter picks in this guide. If your pet has been diagnosed with kidney disease, the diet should come from your veterinarian, not a shelf.

**Do senior pets need more or less protein as they age — and will high protein hurt my pet's kidneys?**

This is where dogs and cats diverge. The AAHA guidelines describe healthy senior cats as often needing higher-protein, high-digestibility diets to preserve lean muscle, while senior dogs generally need moderated calories with adequate, high-quality protein. On the kidney worry: in healthy pets, high-quality protein is not shown to cause kidney disease. Protein restriction is a management tool after a CKD diagnosis, not a preventive measure for a healthy animal. If your pet has diagnosed kidney disease, protein and phosphorus levels are a veterinary decision.

**Is wet food or dry kibble better for an older pet with dental problems or a poor appetite?**

Soft and wet formats earn their place as teeth wear and appetite fades, especially for cats. Cornell and the AAFP both note that older cats benefit from moisture and easy-to-eat textures, which is why a soft option like the Royal Canin Aging 12+ Thin Slices in Gravy can keep a dental-declined cat eating. Many owners do well pairing a high-protein dry diet with a wet food for hydration. For dogs, a wet topper or stew can revive a flagging appetite. The format is less important than whether the pet will actually eat enough — a pet that stops eating needs a veterinarian, not just a new texture.

**Do I really need a glucosamine/omega-3 "joint support" formula, or should I just give a separate supplement?**

The honest answer is that the in-food joint blend is rarely the deciding factor. The Merck Veterinary Manual points to omega-3 fatty acids and weight control as the strongest joint-support levers, with glucosamine and chondroitin carrying weaker support. A senior diet that includes omega-3 is a reasonable convenience, but the amounts in food are often modest, and a pet with diagnosed arthritis may need a dedicated supplement or medication dosed by a veterinarian. Keeping the pet lean does more than any joint label — and a separate supplement, if used, is a conversation to have with your vet.
