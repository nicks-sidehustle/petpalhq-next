A new rabbit goes wrong, in this guide's editorial judgment, for one reason more than any other: the cage is treated as the whole answer. A first-time owner buys a hutch, puts the rabbit in it, adds a bowl of pellets, and assumes the job is done. It is not. The cage is a home base — a place to sleep and retreat — but a rabbit kept in one all day is a bored, unhealthy animal. What actually keeps a rabbit well is a hay-first diet, hours of daily exercise outside the cage, a litter habit, water it will actually drink, and a place to hide. The gear is only the frame around those habits.

Start with the diet, because it is the thing most new owners get wrong. A rabbit is a grazing animal built to eat grass all day, and unlimited hay is roughly 80% of a healthy diet — not pellets, which are a small measured supplement, and not an endless supply of veggies. Hay keeps a rabbit's sensitive gut moving and grinds down teeth that never stop growing. Any change in food has to happen slowly, over days, because a sudden switch can make a rabbit dangerously ill. If you take one thing from this guide, take this: keep grass hay in front of the rabbit at all times, and treat everything else as secondary.

Then build the home outward from the cage. The home base gives the rabbit somewhere secure to sleep and be shut in when you are out. A hay rack keeps the forage clean over a litter pan, which is how litter training clicks. Water goes in two vessels — a spill-proof bottle and an open bowl — because many rabbits drink more freely from a bowl. An exercise pen provides the daily run time that a cage cannot, and a wooden hideout gives a prey animal the retreat it needs to relax. The picks below are that kit, in setup order, not seven cages ranked against each other. Where a claim rests on a maker's listing or on welfare consensus, that source is named, because PetPalHQ does not run a testing lab.

One principle sits above the whole setup and never bends. A cage is a home base, not a life. A rabbit needs several hours of supervised floor time every single day, in a pen or a rabbit-proofed room, and that time is only safe once you have bunny-proofed the cords and hazards a rabbit will chew. Rabbits are social, eight-to-twelve-year prey animals, best kept spayed or neutered and in a bonded pair — not a low-effort starter pet, and never an Easter impulse. Set the home up around those truths before the rabbit comes home, and the first week starts the way it should.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What do I actually need to buy before bringing a rabbit home?**
A: Set up six things before the rabbit arrives, and think of them as habits with gear attached rather than a shopping list. First, a home base cage roomy enough to sleep, eat, and retreat in, understood as the bedroom rather than the whole house. Second, and most important, a large supply of grass hay such as Timothy, because unlimited hay is roughly 80% of a healthy rabbit's diet. Third, a hay feeder rack to keep that hay clean and off the floor. Fourth, water offered two ways — a spill-proof bottle plus a heavy bowl, since many rabbits drink more from a bowl. Fifth, a corner litter pan filled with paper litter and hay so you can litter-train from day one. Sixth, an exercise pen for the hours of daily floor time a cage cannot provide, and a hideout so a prey animal has somewhere to feel safe. Having all of this ready before the rabbit comes home makes the first week far calmer for both of you.

**Q: Is a cage enough, or does my rabbit really need time outside it?**
A: A cage is never enough on its own, and this is the point new owners most often miss. A cage is a home base — a secure place to sleep and be shut in while you are out — but a rabbit kept in one around the clock becomes bored, unhealthy, and unhappy. Rabbits need several hours of exercise every single day, outside the cage, in a pen or a rabbit-proofed room, where they can run, dig, stretch out, and do the happy mid-air twists called binkies. This daily floor time is not an optional extra or a reward; it is a basic welfare requirement, as fundamental as food and water. If your home or schedule cannot make room for daily out-of-cage time, a rabbit is not the right pet. Before every session, bunny-proof the space by covering or blocking electrical cords, which rabbits chew and which can seriously injure them.

**Q: What should a rabbit eat?**
A: The core of a rabbit's diet is unlimited grass hay, such as Timothy, which should make up roughly 80% of what it eats and be available at all times. Hay provides the fiber that keeps a rabbit's gut moving and the constant chewing that wears down teeth which never stop growing, so it does far more than fill the stomach. Around that core, a rabbit gets a small measured daily portion of pellets and a serving of fresh leafy greens, but these are the supporting cast, not the main meal — too many pellets or treats and too little hay is a common and harmful mistake. Fresh water must always be available. One rule matters as much as the ingredients: change any food gradually over several days, because a rabbit's digestive system is sensitive and a sudden switch can cause serious illness. Young rabbits are usually raised on alfalfa and move to grass hay as adults, so match the diet to the rabbit's age and your vet's advice.

**Q: How do I litter-train a rabbit, and what litter is safe?**
A: Rabbits litter-train more easily than most people expect, because they naturally choose one corner to toilet in. Put a corner litter pan in the spot the rabbit picks, and place a hay feeder directly above it — rabbits like to eat and toilet at the same time, so hay over the box makes training click quickly. Fill the pan correctly: a layer of paper-based litter on the bottom with a generous handful of hay on top. What you must never use is clumping cat litter, which can cause a fatal internal blockage if eaten, or softwood shavings like pine and cedar, which are linked to respiratory and liver problems. Expect a few early accidents and be willing to move the box to wherever the rabbit actually goes. Training is fastest and most reliable in a spayed or neutered rabbit, since intact rabbits mark territory, and the box needs emptying daily or the rabbit will stop using it.

**Q: Are rabbits good pets for young children?**
A: Rabbits are often marketed as easy children's pets, but they are usually a poor fit for young kids, and it is worth being honest about that before buying. Rabbits are delicate prey animals that generally dislike being picked up and can be injured by rough or well-meaning handling, so they rarely become the cuddly lap pet a child imagines. They are also a serious long-term commitment — they live eight to twelve years, are social animals that do best in a bonded pair, need daily exercise and specialized care, and require a rabbit-savvy exotic vet who is rarer and costlier than a typical clinic. The impulse holiday purchase, especially around Easter, is how many rabbits end up surrendered once the reality sets in. A rabbit can be a wonderful family pet when an adult takes primary responsibility and children are taught calm, ground-level interaction, but it should never be bought on a whim as a low-effort starter animal.
