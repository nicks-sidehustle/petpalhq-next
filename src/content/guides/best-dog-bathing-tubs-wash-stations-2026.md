## Safety: The Two Bodies at Risk

A wash station changes two risk pictures at once — the dog's and yours — so it earns a dedicated safety section covering both.

**Water temperature.** Keep bath water lukewarm, roughly 95 to 100°F, and never hot. Dogs have thinner skin than people and scald far more easily, and these stations tie straight into your household hot water, so the failure is easy to reach. Test the water on the inside of your wrist before you rinse the dog, and set the hot-side mixing valve conservatively so a slipped handle cannot deliver a scald.

**Restraint, and the tether trap.** Use the grooming loop or a leash to keep the dog in place, but never leave a tethered dog unattended for even a moment. A dog that jumps or slips off the edge can hang itself on the loop — this is the most serious hazard in the whole workflow. Prefer a quick-release tether, and keep one hand on the dog whenever it is secured.

**Slip.** Wet stainless is dangerously slick. The PE filter board or a rubber non-slip mat inside the basin is mandatory, not optional. The single most common owner complaint in this class is a slippery entry ramp, so run grip tape or a mat on the ramp too — a dog that slips on the way in learns to dread the tub.

**Your back — the whole point, and how it backfires.** A raised 30-to-40-inch working height stops the bend-and-hoist injuries you get bathing a dog in a floor tub. But lifting a large dog up into a 50-inch basin reintroduces exactly that injury. For any dog over roughly 40 pounds, choose a walk-in ramp or access-stairs model — the VEVOR 50-inch or the Garvee 46-inch — and let the dog climb in on its own feet.

**Corrosion and electrical.** Door hardware and corner welds rusting is documented across VEVOR and its peers, so dry the hardware after each use. Water and electricity share this space: keep the required GFCI-protected outlet and any dryer well away from the spray zone, and run a drip loop on every cord so water cannot track down into the plug.

**Leaks.** Several tubs in this class weep at the welds or drain after use, and a wet floor around a stainless tub is its own slip hazard. Seat the drain kit carefully, and check for pooling before it becomes a fall.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What stainless steel grade and gauge should a dog grooming tub be?**

Buy 304 stainless, and treat the gauge as secondary. Direct Animal's material guidance names type 304 as the industry standard because its chromium content wards off corrosion and its non-porous surface sanitizes between dogs — the two things that matter in a tub washed daily. Gauge measures thickness, where a lower number is thicker: the VEVOR 50-inch is described as 16-gauge, which is sturdy, while many entry tubs use lighter 18-gauge. The grade decides whether the tub resists rust; the gauge decides how much abuse the basin shrugs off. A thin 304 tub still beats a thick tub in a cheaper alloy, so start with the grade.

**How do I stop a stainless dog wash station from rusting at the door and corners?**

Dry the metal after every bath — this is the whole answer, and owners who skip it are the ones reporting corner and door-track rust within weeks. Even 304 stainless corrodes at welds and hardware when it sits wet, especially in a humid garage or basement. Wipe the door track, corners, and fittings dry after each use, keep the station up off a wet floor, and do not let it drip-dry overnight. If a wipe-down is not realistic in your routine, buy the fully welded KANIS body, which is built to resist the seam corrosion the bolted-panel tubs are prone to.

**What tub length do I need for a large-breed or long-backed dog?**

Match the basin to the dog before anything else. A 50-inch tub, like the VEVOR 50-inch or the KANIS, fits most medium-to-large breeds with room to turn. The 46-inch Garvee handles medium-to-large dogs but not giant breeds — a Great Dane or Mastiff wants a 50-to-62-inch tub. The 34-inch compact sink is for cats and small dogs only, and no amount of coaxing fits a Labrador into it. Waggz's buying guide flags mismatched length as the mistake owners make most, and it is unrecoverable: a tub too short for the dog is unusable no matter how well it is built.

**Are walk-in ramps or access stairs safer than lifting my dog into the tub?**

Yes, for any dog you cannot comfortably lift. PetEdge names walk-in stair or ramp entry as the standard back-saving feature precisely because lifting a wet large or arthritic dog up into a fixed basin is how owners hurt their backs — the same injury the station was bought to prevent. Ramps suit dogs that walk up an incline easily; access stairs, like the Garvee's, suit stiff seniors that manage steps better than a slope. For any dog over roughly 40 pounds, treat walk-in entry as mandatory. Always add grip tape or a mat to the ramp, since a slick ramp is the top owner complaint in this class.

**How do I plumb a dog washing station, and where does it drain?**

Most of these stations arrive ready to connect like a utility sink. They ship with a hot-and-cold faucet and a showerhead or pull-out sprayer, so you supply hot and cold lines the way you would for a laundry sink, and the basin drains through an included drain kit into your household waste line or a nearby floor drain. Seat the drain kit carefully — several tubs in this class leak at the drain after use, and a wet floor is a slip hazard. Because water and electricity share the workspace, run the station near a GFCI-protected outlet, keep any dryer clear of the spray zone, and put a drip loop on every cord.

**Is a $600-to-$1,600 professional station worth it over a plastic booster bath or my own bathtub?**

It depends entirely on your dog's size and how often you bathe. For a groomer, a multi-dog home, or an owner bathing a big shedding breed monthly, a raised walk-in station pays for itself in a spared back and a tub that sanitizes between dogs — a plastic booster bath is porous and cannot match that. For a single small dog bathed a few times a year, it does not: a non-slip mat in the family bathtub, or at most the compact 34-inch stainless sink, does the job for far less. Buy up the tier only when frequency and dog size justify it.
